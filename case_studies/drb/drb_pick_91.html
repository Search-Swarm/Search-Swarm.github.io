Light-Based Therapies in Aesthetic Medicine: Current Applications and Recent Scientific Advancements (2020–2026)

Light-based therapies encompass three principal technologies used in aesthetic dermatology: monochromatic lasers, polychromatic intense pulsed light (IPL), and non-coherent light-emitting diodes (LEDs) operating via photobiomodulation (PBM). Research published between 2020 and early 2026 demonstrates convergence on several themes: safer pigment clearance using longer wavelengths and lower energies for skin of color; hybrid multi-wavelength platforms delivering ablative and non-ablative effects simultaneously; and combination strategies pairing light devices with topical depigmenting agents rather than relying on monotherapy.

---

1. Laser Therapy

1.1 Photoaging Treatment

Fractional ablative lasers remain the gold standard for moderate-to-severe photoaging. Fractional CO₂ (10,600 nm) achieves approximately 70% reduction in wrinkles and ~85% improvement in overall skin quality [1], though downtime averages 7–14 days. Fractional Er:YAG (2,940 nm) delivers comparable efficacy with shorter healing (~3–7 days) due to minimal collateral thermal damage [1].

Non-ablative fractional lasers (Nd:YAG at 1,320 nm; diode lasers at 1,440/1,927 nm; thulium at 1,927 nm) stimulate collagen remodeling without epidermal disruption and typically require less than 48 hours of downtime [1][2].

1.2 Hyperpigmentation — Age Spots / Solar Lentigines

Picosecond Nd:YAG lasers have emerged as the preferred modality over traditional Q-switched nanosecond lasers:

  • 1064-nm picosecond Nd:YAG: Bernstein et al. demonstrated significant mMASI improvement at both 3 and 8 months post-treatment [3]. Chan et al. achieved a 75% total mMASI decrease at 6 weeks in Fitzpatrick III–IV patients with no hypo- or hyperpigmentation [3].
  • 532-nm picosecond Nd:YAG: Achieves 93.02% excellent-to-great improvement with significantly lower PIH (~5%) versus ~30% with conventional Q-switched Nd:YAG at 532 nm [4].
  • 730-nm picosecond laser: Achieves 78.4% clearance after two sessions with 90% patient satisfaction at 6-month follow-up [3].

The mechanism involves laser-induced optical breakdown (LIOB) — a photoacoustic rather than purely photothermal effect generated by ultra-short picosecond pulses that shatters melanin clusters into smaller particles with minimal thermal diffusion [3][5]. Histological studies using 3D melanocyte-containing skin models confirmed LIOB triggers dermal remodeling cascades including upregulation of MMPs (MMP-1, -3, -9), collagens (COL1A1, COL3A1), and heat shock proteins [5].

A 2025 meta-analysis (Wu et al., 21 studies, 971 patients) found picosecond lasers had significantly less PIH (p = 0.02), less PIH hypopigmentation (p = 0.002), lower pain scores (VAS p < 0.01), and comparable endogenous hyperpigmentation clearance versus nanosecond lasers (RR 1.06) [6].

1.3 Melasma & Skin Brightening

Melasma remains one of the most challenging indications due to high recurrence rates.

Low-fluence Q-Switched Nd:YAG (1064 nm): Remains the most established laser-based brightening approach. Weekly/biweekly treatments at 1–3 J/cm² using large spot sizes (6–10 mm) induce subthreshold photoacoustic/photo-mechanical disruption of melanosomes ("subcellular selective photothermolysis") without destroying melanocyte membranes [7]. Typically requires 5–15 sessions but carries high recurrence rates (64–81% at 3 months) [8].

Picosecond Nd:YAG (1064 nm): Significant improvements documented even in Fitzpatrick IV–V skin [3][9]. However, a 2026 systematic review (Cureus, 52 RCTs) found non-significant pooled improvement for laser-based therapies for melasma overall (MD 0.70, 95% CI −0.55 to 1.95, p = 0.2682), underscoring limitations of existing evidence [10].

Critical caveat: Laser monotherapy may worsen melasma or cause rebound lesions after discontinuation [8]. Combination therapy with topical agents is strongly preferred [8].

1.4 Hybrid Fractional Lasers (Key Recent Advance)

The Sciton Halo system delivers coincident fractional Er:YAG (2,940 nm) and non-ablative 1,470 nm infrared simultaneously — ablative pigment clearance combined with deep collagen stimulation — achieving approximately 80% improvement with median downtime of only 3 days [11].

The concept of minimal photothermolysis (Garg et al., 2024) uses spot sizes and fluences below the conventional threshold (<3 J/cm²) for treating dermal hyperpigmentation in skin of color, achieving clinical improvement with reduced risk [12].

2. Intense Pulsed Light (IPL)

2.1 Technology Fundamentals

IPL emits non-coherent polychromatic light (typically 500–1200 nm), enabling simultaneous targeting of melanin, hemoglobin, and water through interchangeable filters [13].

2.2 Current Platforms

Lumenis Stellar M22 with XPL™ Technology (launched November 2025): Features 8 expert filters, sub-pulsing technology, Advanced Pulse Design, Glide Mode for faster treatments, and 850+ built-in protocols. Clinical data showed ≥70% improvement in a single session with 76–100% clearance of vascular/pigmented lesions [14].

InMode Lumecca IPL: Delivers up to 3× more energy in the 500–600 nm range with peak power of 3.3 kW/cm². Available in variants optimized for lighter (515–1200 nm) or darker skin types (580–1200 nm), achieving results in 1–3 sessions versus 4–6 for traditional IPL [15].

2.3 Solar Lentigines (Age Spots)

IPL achieves success rates of 74.6–94.4% depending on parameters and anatomical location [4], generally offering the best balance of efficacy and safety for lentigines compared to single-wavelength lasers [4].

2.4 Melasma

IPL works better for epidermal melasma than dermal deposits [16][17]. A 2024 Pakistani study (n = 62) comparing IPL (560 nm filter) versus intradermal tranexamic acid found IPL achieved 57.1% mean mMASI reduction vs. 42.2% for TXA (p = 0.0034), with milder side effects [18]. Outcomes converged at 3 months (p = 0.29), suggesting IPL provides faster initial improvement but maintenance requires adjunctive therapy [18].

Guidelines strongly recommend combining IPL with topical lightening agents for optimal outcomes [8].

3. LED Photobiomodulation (PBM)

3.1 Mechanism of Action

LED therapy operates through absorption by mitochondrial cytochrome c oxidase, leading to increased ATP production, TGF-β/SMAD pathway activation (stimulating procollagen expression), MMP downregulation, and tyrosinase/MITF modulation [19][20].

3.2 Key Wavelengths and Evidence

Yellow/Amber LEDs (570–590 nm):

  • Yi et al. (2025, RCT, n = 30): Yellow light combination was superior to red/infrared-only LED for reducing brown spots and improving wrinkles [21].
  • Amber/yellow LED has been identified as effective for reducing melanin content through tyrosinase downregulation [22].

Red LEDs (630 ± 10 nm):

  • Lee et al. (2007, RCT, n = 76): Landmark trial showing 26–36% significant wrinkle reduction with histological confirmation of increased collagen and elastic fibers adjacent to highly active fibroblasts [19].

Near-Infrared LEDs (830–850 nm):

  • Kim et al. (2012): Irradiation decreased melanin synthesis without cytotoxic effects in melanocyte cultures through tyrosinase, TRP-1, and MITF downregulation [20].

3.3 Professional vs. Home Devices

Professional devices deliver higher power densities (30–100 mW/cm²); home-use masks operate at lower intensities (~10 mW/cm² max). The JAAD 2025 consensus confirmed red light PBM does not induce DNA damage even at fluences up to 1280 J/cm² [23].

---

4. Combination Approaches and Synergistic Technologies

4.1 Laser + Topical Agents

Combination of light devices with tranexamic acid, niacinamide, or other depigmenting agents shows consistent superiority over monotherapy. A 2024 RCT found picosecond laser + TXA via microchannels (from fractional mode) produced greater melasma lightening than either modality alone, attributed to enhanced trans-epidermal drug delivery [24]. A 2026 meta-analysis (11 RCTs) found combination therapy with topical agents showed statistically significant advantage at 8 weeks (SMD: −0.58, p = 0.004) [25].

4.2 Radiofrequency (RF) + Light Hybrids

RF microneedling is inherently safe for all Fitzpatrick types because its electrothermal mechanism targets interstitial fluid, not melanin [2][26]. The Lumenis Polaris WRA (diode 900 nm + bipolar RF) showed greater dermal collagen improvement in a split-face design (64.3% good/excellent) [27]. A combined needle RF + IPL device for acne showed 34.8% mean lesion reduction at 12 weeks (p < 0.05) [28].

4.3 675-nm Laser

A 2025 study in 28 Thai patients (85.71% FST IV) using 675-nm monotherapy (RedTouch®) showed 41% mMASI reduction at 3 months (p < 0.0001) with no PIH, only mild transient erythema, and no serious adverse events [29]. This represents a promising option for darker skin types.

---

5. Safety for Darker Skin Tones (Fitzpatrick IV–VI)

Key strategies include: use of longer wavelengths (1064 nm, 675 nm); sub-threshold energy (minimal photothermolysis, <3 J/cm²); pre-treatment with hydroquinone 2% (≥2 weeks) or retinoids; and test patches before full treatment [2][12]. The 1,064-nm picosecond laser and 675-nm 675-nm laser are the most supported options for FST IV–V, with no reported cases of hypo- or hyperpigmentation in available case series [3][29][30].

---

6. Limitations

  • No large RCTs exist for LED monotherapy for melasma as a primary endpoint [20][22].
  • Long-term data (>1 year) for picosection lasers in melasma management remains sparse [7].
  • The 2026 Cureus meta-analysis found non-significant overall improvement for laser-based melasma therapies, with low certainty of evidence [10].
  • Underpowered melasma trials (e.g., the Galache 2025 pilot RCT for amber LED vs. TXA was 51% powered) [22].
  • Standardized treatment parameters across the field remain an unmet need.

Laser Therapy in Aesthetic Medicine: Current State and Recent Scientific Advancements (2020–2026)

Overview

Laser therapy has become a cornerstone of aesthetic dermatology, offering minimally invasive approaches for photoaging, skin whitening/brightening, and hyperpigmentation treatment. Recent advances include picosecond laser technology, hybrid fractional devices, novel wavelengths (730 nm), low-fluence approaches, and combination therapies that improve efficacy while minimizing adverse effects.

---

1. PHOTOAGING TREATMENT

Mechanisms of Action

Photoaging results from cumulative UV radiation damage causing collagen degradation, elastin fragmentation, reduced collagen synthesis, and abnormal collagen deposition. Laser therapy reverses these changes primarily through thermal stimulation that induces:

  • Immediate collagen contraction — denatured collagen fibers shrink to approximately one-third their original length when heated above 43–60°C [1][2]
  • Neocollagenesis — sustained thermal stimulation activates fibroblasts to produce new collagen types I and III over weeks to months [3][4]
  • Matrix remodeling — heat shock protein activation triggers local immune responses leading to organized collagen deposition [4]

Clinical outcomes: Fractional CO₂ achieves approximately 70% reduction in wrinkles and 85% improvement in overall skin quality [5]. Fractional Er:YAG offers comparable efficacy with shorter downtime (~3–7 days vs. ~7–14 days for CO₂). Hybrid Fractional Lasers

The Halo™ system (Sciton) delivers coincident fractional Er:YAG (2940 nm) and nonablative 1470 nm pulses simultaneously. The Er:YAG component targets superficial pigment via ablation while the 1470 nm wavelength stimulates deep collagen through non-ablative heating [9][10]. Clinical studies report significant improvement in 80% of patients, especially on dyschromia, with median downtime of only 3 days [9].

Nd:YAG Micropulse Technology

Devices like Aerolase combine Er:YAG micro-peeling with Nd:YAG micropulse modes. Subcoagulative heating at 1064 nm stimulates heat shock proteins and neocollagenogenesis, predominantly increasing collagen types I and III expression areas [4]. Clinical cases showed statistically significant improvements in collagen density (p < 0.05) in patients aged 38–61 [4].

Recent Technological Developments

  • Fractional CO₂ combined with radiofrequency: Studies show synergistic collagen remodeling effects when bipolar RF is combined with ablative fractional CO₂ [6].
  • Hybrid fractional lasers combining ablative + non-ablative wavelengths offer enhanced efficacy with reduced downtime compared to traditional single-wavelength approaches [5][9].
  • Microsecond pulse Nd:YAG: Emerging devices deliver nanosecond-range pulses at higher energies for targeted chromophore destruction with minimal thermal diffusion [4].

2. SKIN WHITENING / BRIGHTENING

Mechanisms of Action

Skin whitening operates through multiple pathways:

  • Selective photothermolysis targeting melanin-containing cells (melanocytes, keratinocytes)
  • Epidermal turnover stimulation promoting desquamation of pigmented keratinocytes
  • Collagen remodeling improving skin radiance and optical properties

Specific Laser Technologies

Q-Switched Nd:YAG Laser (Low-Fluence/Laser Toning)

This remains the most established laser-based approach for skin brightening. Low-fluence Q-switched Nd:YAG (LFQSNY) involves weekly/biweekly treatments at 1–3 J/cm² using large spot sizes (6–10 mm) at 1064 nm [11][12].

Mechanism: Subthreshold energy induces photoacoustic/photo-mechanical disruption of melanosomes ("subcellular selective photothermolysis") without destroying melanocyte membranes [11].

Clinical outcomes: Typically requires 5–15 sessions for favorable results [11]. Benefits include gradual pigment lightening akin to chemical peels with minimal inflammation [12].

Picosecond Lasers (Nd:YAG 1064 nm, Alexandrite 755 nm)

Picosecond lasers deliver ultra-short pulses (picosecond range, 10⁻¹² seconds) generating photoacoustic rather than purely photothermal effects [13][14].

Clinical outcomes: Multiple studies demonstrate significant improvements:

  • Bernstein et al.: Fractional Nd:YAG (1064 nm) PS laser achieved significant mMASI improvement at 3 and 8 months post-treatment with high patient satisfaction [14]
  • Chan et al.: Fractional Nd:YAG PS laser achieved 75% total mMASI decrease at 6 weeks and 66.7% at 12 weeks in Fitzpatrick III–IV patients [14]
  • Liang et al.: Non-fractional PS Nd:YAG was more effective than PS alexandrite (755 nm) for melasma; highest patient satisfaction [14]
Intense Pulsed Light (IPL)

Broad-spectrum light (500–1200 nm) targeting melanin and hemoglobin [1]. Effective for diffuse hyperpigmentation and overall skin tone correction [15].

Clinical outcomes: IPL combined with topical tranexamic acid achieved 50.52% mMASI reduction vs. 38.49% with IPL alone (p < 0.05), plus superior improvement in ultraviolet spots and brown spots [16].

Combination Approaches for Brightening

Recent clinical trials demonstrate that combining lasers with topical agents produces superior brightening outcomes:

  • IPL + topical TXA significantly outperformed IPL alone in reducing melanin index and preventing PIH [16]
  • Fractional laser + tranexamic acid delivery methods shows enhanced efficacy for refractory melasma [17]

3. HYPERPIGMENTATION (AGE SPOTS, MELASMA)

Age Spots / Solar Lentigines

Q-Switched Lasers (Gold Standard)

Efficacy: Q-switched Nd:YAG (532 nm) achieves 76.6% efficacy with 56.6% excellent-to-good improvement [18]. QS ruby laser achieves >75% improvement in 59.34% of patients after one session [18].

Safety concerns: Higher PIH rates compared to newer technologies (up to 30% with QS Nd:YAG vs. 5% with picosecond Nd:YAG) [18].

Picosecond Lasers (Emerging Standard)

Efficacy: Picosecond Nd:YAG (532 nm) achieves 93.02% excellent improvement with significantly lower PIH (5% vs. 30%) [18]. The 730 nm picosecond laser achieves 78.4% clearance after two sessions with 90% patient satisfaction at 6-month follow-up [14].

Specific advantages: Photoacoustic fragmentation effectively breaks pigment clusters into smaller particles with minimal thermal effects, reducing inflammation risk [14].

Fractional CO₂ Laser

Despite being effective for photoaging, fractional CO₂ shows inferior results for lentigines compared to Q-switched/picosecond lasers — only 8–23% clearance rates reported [18].

Laser Modalities for Melasma

Low-Fluence Q-Switched Nd:YAG (Laser Toning):

  • Protocol: 5–15 weekly sessions at 1–3 J/cm², 1064 nm [11]
  • Outcomes: Variable success; high recurrence rates (64–81% at 3 months) [19]
  • Risks: Mottled hypopigmentation, rebound hyperpigmentation (especially in darker skin) [11]

Picosecond Nd:YAG (1064 nm):

  • Feng et al. meta-analysis (2023): PS laser significantly reduced MASI/mMASI overall (P = 0.04 for 1064 nm subgroup) [20]
  • Bernstein et al.: Significant melasma improvement at 3 and 8 months with minimal side effects [14]
  • Chan et al.: 75% mMASI decrease at 6 weeks in Fitzpatrick III–IV patients [14]
  • Limitation: One split-face study found no superiority over LFQSNY for pigment lightening [11]

Fractional Picosecond Nd:YAG (1064 nm):

  • Chan et al.: Well-tolerated with no hypo/hyperpigmentation in Asian skin types [14]
  • Histological analysis showed vacuoles in epidermis/dermis and increased collagen type I expression — suggesting dual pigment clearance + dermal remodeling [21]

Non-Fractional Picosecond Alexandrite (755 nm):

  • Manuskiatti et al.: 25% reduction in melasma severity (not statistically superior to fractionated beam) [14]
  • Full beam associated with lower PIH incidence than fractionated beam [14]

Combination Therapies (Recent Advances):

  1. PS Nd:YAG + Thulium (1927 nm): Simultaneous treatment addressing both pigment migration and dermal remodeling [14]
  2. PS Nd:YAG + Tranexamic Acid: Enhanced efficacy through anti-angiogenic and tyrosinase-inhibitory mechanisms [17]
  3. Q-switched Nd:YAG + Micropulse Nd:YAG: Combined vascular coagulation with collagen remodeling [4]
  4. IPL + Topical TXA: Superior to IPL alone (−50.52% vs −38.49% mMASI reduction; p < 0.05) [16]
Critical Caveats for Melasma Treatment
  • Laser monotherapy may worsen melasma or cause rebound lesions after discontinuation [19]
  • Ablative fractional lasers are NOT recommended as monotherapy for melasma [19]
  • Combination therapy with topical agents is strongly preferred [19][22]
  • Maintenance therapy with ongoing topical agents is essential to sustain results [19]

LIMITATIONS AND GAPS

  1. High heterogeneity exists across melasma laser studies (I² = 70%), limiting definitive conclusions about comparative efficacy [20]
  2. Long-term data (>1 year) for picosecond lasers in melasma management remains sparse [11]
  3. Standardization of protocols (fluence, number of sessions, interval) varies widely across studies
  4. Few head-to-head comparisons between picosecond and nanosecond lasers exist for melasma specifically
  5. Skin whitening/brightening lacks robust RCT-level evidence; much of the literature relies on observational studies and expert consensus

Intense Pulsed Light (IPL) Therapy in Aesthetic Medicine: Current State and Recent Advancements

1. Technology Fundamentals

Wavelength Ranges

IPL devices emit non-coherent, polychromatic light with a broad spectrum. The standard emission range is 500–1200 nm (or 400–1400 nm in some devices) [1][2]. This allows simultaneous targeting of multiple chromophores — melanin, hemoglobin, and water. Key cut-off filters available on modern devices include: 515, 550, 560, 570, 590, 615, 645, 690, and 755 nm [2]. The 515–560 nm range is most commonly used for general photorejuvenation, while 515 or 580 nm handpieces are selected based on skin type (515 nm for lighter skin, 580 nm for Fitzpatrick types III–IV) [3][4].

For specific applications:

  • Pigmented lesions/photoaging: 630–1100 nm (melanin absorption) [2]
  • Vascular lesions: 500–600 nm (hemoglobin absorption) [2]
  • Collagen remodeling/dermal heating: 1200+ nm (water absorption) [2]

Key Parameters

  • Fluence: Up to 40 J/cm²; typically 10–18 J/cm² for clinical use (12–18 for types I–III, 10–15 for type IV) [1][3]
  • Pulse duration: 5–100 ms, based on thermal relaxation time of target chromophore [1][2]
  • Pulse delay/interval: 10–20 ms for single-pulse, up to 400+ ms for multi-pulse configurations [1]
  • Spot size: Varies; larger spot sizes penetrate deeper [2]

2. Devices

Established Devices

  • Lumenis M22/Stellar M22: Multi-application platform offering IPL + Nd:YAG laser in one system. Features 8 filters and advanced sub-pulsing technology [5][6].
  • InMode Lumecca/Lumecca IPL: Delivers up to 3× more energy in the 500–600 nm range with high peak power (3.3 kW/cm²). Available in two handpiece variants: 515–1200 nm (skin types I–II) and 580–1200 nm (skin types III–IV). Achieves results in 1–3 sessions vs. 4–6 for traditional IPL [3][4].
  • Sciton BBL (BroadBand Light): Sixth-generation photon technology; sixth generation of broadband light [7].

Newer Technologies (2024–2026)

  • Lumenis Stellar M22 with XPL™ (Expert Pulsed Light) Technology (launched November 2025): Features 8 expert filters, sub-pulsing technology, Advanced Pulse Design, Glide Mode for faster treatments, and 850+ built-in protocols. Clinical data showed ≥70% improvement in a single session and 76–100% clearance of vascular/pigmented lesions after one session [5][6].
  • Narrowband IPL (450–600 nm) with Advanced Fluorescence Technology (AFT): Used in large retrospective studies (n=100) achieving GAIS scores of 8.02 (vascular) and 8.14 (pigmented) with excellent safety [8].
  • Pulse Configuration Manipulation: New IPL systems integrate three different pulse configurations to balance safety and efficacy. Studies show no serious adverse events with effective treatment across acne, hair removal, rosacea, pigmentation, and vascular lesions [9].

3. Photoaging Treatment

Mechanism of Action

IPL induces controlled thermal damage to the dermis, triggering:

  • Collagen remodeling: Heating of collagen fibers causes immediate contraction and stimulates neocollagenesis (types I and III) over subsequent months [2][10].
  • Elastin reorganization: Improved tissue architecture [10].
  • Epidermal turnover: Enhanced keratinocyte differentiation [2].

Clinical Evidence

A landmark real-world study of 236 patients treated with Lumenis M22 IPL over 6+ sessions demonstrated significant improvements using VISIA imaging:

  • Erythema area decreased by median 210.97 mm² (p<0.001)
  • Pigmentation area decreased by median 192.64 mm² (p<0.001)
  • Wrinkle index improved by median 123.5 (p<0.001)
  • Regular treatment intervals were OR=13.62 predictor of good response; total sessions OR=3.80 [3]

Important caveat: A histological study using six sessions over three months found no statistically significant changes in collagen types I, III, VII, elastin, or tropoelastin at the molecular level, though 50% of volunteers reported mild skin texture improvement [10]. This highlights that clinical improvements may not always correlate with measurable histological changes.

An IPL-PDT (photodynamic therapy) study using 5-ALA showed significantly greater improvements in hydration, pH normalization, and TEWL reduction compared to IPL alone (p<0.01), suggesting combination approaches enhance photoaging outcomes [11].

Predictors of Response

  • Regular treatment intervals and higher total session count strongly predict better outcomes [3]
  • Fitzpatrick type IV skin shows lower response rates (OR=0.12) [3]
  • Age, gender, and diagnosis were not significant predictors [3]

4. Skin Whitening/Brightening

Mechanism

IPL targets melanin through selective photothermolysis. Thermal energy disrupts melanosomes within keratinocytes, causing microcrust formation that flakes off, revealing brighter skin underneath [2]. Additionally, IPL may downregulate melanogenic activity through thermal effects on melanocytes.

Clinical Evidence

IPL is FDA-approved for hyperpigmentation including lentigines and ephelides [1]. For solar lentigines specifically:

  • IPL with KTP filter: 74.6% success on face, 90% on hands at 1 month [12]
  • IPL with 515-nm filter: >50% improvement in 62% of cases, >75% in 23%, with no PIH [12]
  • Another study: 94.4% pigment reduction with 74.2% average clearance [12]

For overall skin brightening/toning, combination approaches with topical agents (hydroquinone, tranexamic acid, chemical peels) yield superior results [13][14].

6. Melasma

Complex Picture

Melasma treatment with IPL remains nuanced and somewhat controversial. While IPL can be effective as part of combination therapy, monotherapy carries risks of worsening melasma, particularly in darker skin types.

Meta-Analysis Evidence

A meta-analysis of 8 studies (215 patients) found IPL-based combination therapy significantly reduces MASI scores (SMD = 0.61, p<0.0001) and increases patient satisfaction (RR = 1.44, p=0.0004) [15]. However, these were combination approaches, not IPL alone.

Epidermal vs. Dermal Melasma

BBL/IPL works better for epidermal melasma than dermal [16][17]. This aligns with the broader literature showing that superficial pigment responds better to broadband light than deep dermal deposits [16].

Clinical Study: IPL vs. Tranexamic Acid

A 2024 Pakistani study (n=62) comparing IPL (560 nm filter) vs. intradermal tranexamic acid found:

  • IPL achieved 57.1% mean mMASI reduction vs. 42.2% for TXA (p=0.0034) [18]
  • More patients achieved moderate-to-significant improvement with IPL (68.8% vs. 0%) [18]
  • Side effects were milder with IPL (59% no side effects) vs. TXA (60% experienced localized bumps, redness, pain) [18]
  • However, post-treatment outcomes converged at 3 months (p=0.29) [18]

Combination Therapy Approaches

The MDPI review (2024) recommends combining energy-based devices with topical lightening agents for optimal outcomes: higher response rates, shorter treatment duration, lower side effects, and reduced recurrence [13]. Specific combinations supported by evidence include:

  • IPL + triple combination cream (TCC) + sunscreen [17]
  • IPL + tranexamic acid (intradermal or oral) [7][18]
  • IPL + Q-switched ruby laser [17]
  • IPL + chemical peels (Jessner's, glycolic acid) [13]

Safety Concerns

Despite favorable safety profiles in controlled settings, concerns remain about IPL exacerbating melasma in susceptible individuals. Some practitioners caution against IPL for melasma entirely, particularly with aggressive parameters [19]. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation risk increases with fluence and is higher in darker skin types [1][3].

IPL vs. Pulsed Dye Laser (PDL) for Rosacea

A 2024 meta-analysis (4 studies, 141 participants) found:

  • Clearance >50%: No significant difference (RR = −0.07, 95% CI: −0.19 to 0.05) [20]

LED Light Therapy in Aesthetic Medicine: Photoaging, Skin Whitening, and Hyperpigmentation

Executive Summary

LED phototherapy represents one of the fastest-growing modalities in aesthetic dermatology. Based on extensive peer-reviewed literature, red light (630nm), near-infrared light (830–850nm), and yellow/amber light (585–590nm) are the primary wavelengths used for photoaging, skin brightening, and hyperpigmentation management. The underlying mechanism is photobiomodulation (PBM), which works through mitochondrial cytochrome c oxidase activation, leading to increased ATP production, modulation of signaling pathways (TGF-β/SMAD, MITF/tyrosinase), and reduction of matrix metalloproteinases. While the evidence base is growing, it remains characterized by small sample sizes, and large-scale randomized controlled trials are still needed to establish definitive clinical guidelines.

---

1. Photoaging Treatment

Wavelengths Used

The most clinically studied and recommended wavelengths for photoaging are:

  • Red (630 ± 10 nm / 620–660 nm) – deepest penetration among visible wavelengths, targets dermal fibroblasts [1][2]
  • Near-infrared (830–850 nm) – penetrates deepest, targets water in deeper dermis [1][2]
  • Yellow/Amber (570–590 nm) – effective for tonal improvement, hyperpigmentation, and inflammation [3][4]

The 2025 Chinese expert consensus recommends red light (630 ± 10 nm, 40–100 mW/cm², 40–100 J/cm²) for photoaging at 2–3 sessions per week [3]. The JAAD 2025 evidence-based consensus confirms PBM uses wavelengths in the red (620–700 nm) and near-infrared (700–1440 nm) spectrum [5].

Clinical Evidence

Lee et al. (2007) conducted a landmark randomized, placebo-controlled, double-blind study with 76 subjects receiving either 830nm LED alone, 633nm LED alone, both sequentially, or sham. All active groups showed statistically significant decreases in mean wrinkle severity (26%, 33%, and 36% respectively). Histological examination demonstrated increased collagen and elastic fibers adjacent to highly active fibroblasts [1][2].

Wunsch & Matuschka (2014) found that after 30 treatment sessions over 15 weeks, patients experienced significantly improved skin complexion, skin feeling, skin roughness, and ultrasonographically measured intradermal collagen density. A 31% increase in collagen density was reported after eight weeks of consistent treatment [6][7].

Yi et al. (2025) compared 570nm and 590nm yellow light combined with red (620nm) and infrared (850nm) LEDs in 30 patients with facial photoaging over 8 weeks. Both treatments significantly reduced fine lines and brown spots. The 570nm combination showed superior efficacy for wrinkles and brown spots specifically [4].

Ngoc et al. (2023) meta-analysis of 31 studies found that yellow LEDs and near-infrared devices showed outstanding effectiveness for skin rejuvenation (I² = 0% and 33% respectively) [8].

Celluma Dior x Lucibel mask (630 ± 10 nm, 15.6 J/cm², 12 min, twice weekly) showed significant improvements in crow's feet wrinkle depth (38.3% after 84 days), skin firmness (23.6%), elasticity (18.7%), dermal density (47.7%), and complexion homogeneity (34.9%) after 3 months [9].

Kwon et al. (2021) demonstrated that combined 630nm LED and 850nm IRED enhanced collagen and elastin deposition in porcine skin models [10].

Mechanisms of Action for Photoaging

The primary mechanism involves photobiomodulation via cytochrome c oxidase (CCO) in mitochondrial respiratory chain complexes. Red and near-infrared photons are absorbed by CCO, resulting in:

  1. ATP production increase – enhanced cellular energy [11]
  2. Nitric oxide (NO) release – reverses NO-mediated inhibition of cellular respiration; NO also acts as a vasodilatory signaling molecule [11]
  3. TGF-β/SMAD pathway activation – stimulates procollagen type I and IV expression; optimal dose at 40 J/cm² for 800nm irradiation [11]
  4. MMP downregulation – decreased matrix metalloproteinase expression preserves existing collagen [12]
  5. Fibroblast proliferation – increased fibroblast activity with concurrent reduction in MMP expression [12]

The 2015 JCAD review noted that "RL is particularly effective in reducing blemishes and dark stains since NIR showed higher effectiveness in improving skin elasticity and wrinkles," recommending combination RL + NIR for optimal photorejuvenation [2].

2. Skin Whitening / Brightening

Wavelengths Used

  • Red (630nm) – most commonly associated with skin brightening effects
  • Near-infrared (830–850nm) – secondary brightening effect
  • Yellow/Amber (590nm) – mentioned for tonal improvement

Clinical Evidence

In studies using LED masks emitting 630nm red and 850nm near-infrared light at 10 mW/cm², approximately one-quarter of participants noticed skin-whitening effects, with some reporting their skin tone became brighter and blemishes faded [13]. The home-use LED mask study (630nm + 850nm, 12 weeks) published in 2025 found 86.2% of patients showed improvement in periorbital wrinkles versus 16.7% in controls, with no specific whitening endpoint but general skin brightening reported [14].

The 2024 integrative review on PBM for melasma concluded that wavelengths including red (630nm), amber (585 and 590nm), and infrared (830 and 850nm) at radiant exposures between 1 and 20 J/cm² "significantly reduce the melanin content" [15].

Mechanisms

Skin whitening/brightening occurs through several interconnected pathways:

  1. Tyrosinase downregulation – reduced enzymatic activity in the melanin synthesis pathway [13][15]
  2. Reduced inflammatory signaling – less inflammation means less stimulation of melanocytes and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation [13]
  3. Enhanced cellular turnover – increased ATP and improved circulation support natural exfoliation, replacing pigmented cells with new ones [13]
  4. MITF pathway modulation – depigmenting effects result from downregulation of microphthalmia-associated transcription factor (MITF) and tyrosinase expression [15][16]

Important Caveats

  • Blue light (400–500nm) should be used cautiously; it has been shown to increase melanin production via opsin-3 expression [16].
  • Green light (532nm) lacks robust clinical evidence for hyperpigmentation treatment; Celluma International states that green wavelengths are "too short to reach the melanocytes in the dermal layer" and no FDA-cleared device exists for pigmentation using green light [17].
  • Effects are gradual and require 8–16+ weeks of consistent use [13].
  • No single RCT has been designed and powered specifically to measure skin whitening as a primary endpoint.

However, special caution is warranted for skin of color, as visible light may produce non-transient hyperpigmentation in darker skin types [12]. Excessive fluence or improper dosing can paradoxically increase ROS and trigger pigmentation [16].

4. Professional vs. Home-Use LED Devices

Professional Devices

Professional-grade devices typically deliver higher power densities (30–100 mW/cm²) and include both red and near-infrared wavelengths simultaneously. Examples include:

  • Omnilux (633nm red + 830nm NIR) – FDA-cleared medical device [23]
  • Lumiphase IR (940nm) – used in Barolet's melasma pilot study [16]
  • Healite – used in professional photobiomodulation settings

Home-Use Devices

Home devices are generally lower power (typically 10 mW/cm² max) and primarily use red (630–660nm) and sometimes near-infrared (830–850nm). FDA clearance indicates these devices are considered safe but does not confirm effectiveness [24]. Key home devices include:

  • Celluma panels (various wavelength combinations)
  • CurrentBody face masks (red + NIR)
  • Therabody TheraFace (648 LEDs in three modes)
  • Shark CryoGlow (red + cryotherapy)
  • Dior x Lucibel (630nm, developed with Celluma) [9]

The 2025 home-use LED mask study (630nm + 850nm, Korean device) demonstrated significant improvement (86.2% vs 16.7% for placebo, p<0.001) in crow's feet wrinkles after 12 weeks of twice-weekly use [14].

Critical Assessment

The American Academy of Dermatology notes that while studies indicate red-light LED devices can produce "subtle to noticeable results," patients should consult a board-certified dermatologist before using home devices and select FDA-cleared products [24]. The Mohs Society recommends choosing FDA-cleared devices and following manufacturer instructions [25].

5. Recent Guidelines and Consensus Statements

JAAD 2025 Evidence-Based Consensus: Established 38 consensus statements supporting PBM as safe for adult patients. Confirmed red light PBM does not induce DNA damage. Supported use for peripheral neuropathy, androgenic alopecia, wound ulcers, decubitus ulcers, and acute radiation dermatitis [5].

Chinese Expert Consensus (2025): Recommended red light (630–690nm, 30–100 mW/cm², 30–100 J/cm²) for acute/chronic wounds; established parameters for various conditions [26].

2025 Consensus on Red/Blue/Yellow Light Therapy in Dermatology: Specifically recommended yellow light (590 ± 10 nm, 20–48 J/cm²) for melasma at 1–2 sessions/week, and red light (630 ± 10 nm) for reducing melanin production [3].

6. Limitations and Future Directions

  1. Small sample sizes: Most clinical trials involve fewer than 30 patients, limiting statistical power.
  2. Heterogeneous protocols: Variability in wavelength, fluence, treatment frequency, and duration makes cross-study comparisons difficult.
  3. Lack of gold-standard RCTs: No large, multi-center, placebo-controlled RCTs have been completed for LED monotherapy for melasma or age spots.
  4. Underpowered melasma trial: The Galache 2025 amber LED vs. TXA trial was only 51% powered and found no significant MASI difference [20].
  5. Standardization needed: The Ngoc et al. meta-analysis noted good consistency for skin rejuvenation (I² = 0%) but acknowledged heterogeneity in other areas [8].
  6. Home-use evidence gap: While home devices are popular, rigorous clinical evidence specifically for home-use regimens is limited.

Future research priorities include: larger multi-center RCTs, standardized treatment parameters, long-term follow-up data, and investigation of combination approaches (LED + topical agents like tranexamic acid or niacinamide).

Emerging Light-Based Technologies in Aesthetic Medicine: Photoaging, Hyperpigmentation, and Skin Brightening

1. RF-Light Hybrid Devices (Radiofrequency Combined with Light/Laser)

RF-light hybrid devices represent a growing area in aesthetic medicine, combining the deep dermal heating of radiofrequency with the surface-level photothermal effects of light/laser technologies.

Existing Hybrid Platforms

The Polaris WRA (Lumenis) is a well-documented combined device pairing a diode laser (900 nm) with bipolar radiofrequency. A split-face study in 14 women (FST II-IV) found that after three treatments, both Polaris WRA and its infrared comparator (ReFirme ST) improved collagen density, but Polaris WRA showed greater histological improvement in dermal collagen on Masson's Trichrome staining [1]. Patient global assessment favored Polaris WRA (64.3% good/excellent vs. 42.8%) [1].

A combined needle RF and vacuum IPL device demonstrated significant efficacy for acne vulgaris (moderate-to-severe): at 12-week follow-up, the RF/IPL-treated side showed 34.80% mean reduction in acne lesions versus 13.76% for the control (p<0.05). Only one patient experienced mild transient erythema [2].

A 2024 review in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology highlighted that hybrid lasers (fractional ablative + non-ablative) and picosecond lasers were featured prominently alongside safer protocols for ethnic skin [3]. The broader RF landscape emphasizes that RF microneedling is inherently safe for darker skin because its electrothermal mechanism bypasses melanin absorption—the chromophores targeted are not melanin, making it suitable for all Fitzpatrick types without pigmentary risk [4].

Key Advantages and Limitations

Hybrid RF-light devices theoretically combine epidermal/superficial remodeling (from light) with deep dermal neocollagenesis (from RF). However, the current evidence base for these hybrids in pigmentary indications specifically remains limited, and comparative studies between different hybrid platforms are sparse. The main clinical evidence comes from small split-face studies and case series rather than large RCTs [2].

2. Picosecond Laser Technology

Picosecond lasers (pulse durations in the picosecond range, 10⁻¹² seconds) have become a major advancement over traditional nanosecond and Q-switched lasers, primarily due to their ability to generate laser-induced optical breakdown (LIOB)—a photoacoustic rather than purely photothermal mechanism.

Mechanism: LIOB and Tissue Remodeling

The LIOB mechanism involves multiphoton ionization where ultra-short, high-intensity pulses create a focused electric field that tears electrons from atoms, generating a plasma bubble and shockwave. This shatters target chromophores (melanin, tattoo ink) through mechanical stress rather than heat diffusion. Histological studies using 3D melanocyte-containing skin models demonstrated intra-epidermal vacuole formation only in melanin-containing models, with upregulation of matrix metalloproteinases (MMP-1, MMP-3, MMP-9), collagens (COL1A1, COL3A1, etc.), and heat shock proteins, confirming that LIOB triggers dermal remodeling cascades [5][6].

Efficacy for Hyperpigmentation and Photoaging

  • 1064 nm picosecond Nd:YAG laser: A 2023 meta-analysis (Feng et al., 2023) found this wavelength significantly reduces melasma severity (MASI/mMASI) with no significant side effects. In contrast, the 755 nm picosecond alexandrite laser was not superior to topical hypopigmentation agents and could cause post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation [7].
  • Fractional 1064 nm Nd:YAG picosecond laser for Asian skin: A 2025 Vietnamese cohort study showed statistically significant improvements in wrinkles, spots, and texture irregularities (70.45% reported moderate improvement), with minimal adverse effects (erythema 68.18%, swelling 29.55%) [8].
  • 532/1064 nm fractional picosecond laser: Ross et al. reported 79% improvement in wrinkles and 93% improvement in pigmentation [9].
  • Acne scars: Split-face trials showed picosecond laser achieved better ECCA score reduction (55% vs. 42% for 1550 nm fractional laser) with less pain [9].
  • Striae distensae: Kaewkes et al. confirmed efficacy and safety of fractional 1064 nm PS laser for mature striae in Fitzpatrick IV-V skin [10].

Picosecond vs. Nanosecond Comparison

A 2025 meta-analysis (Wu et al.) comparing PSLs vs. NSLs across 21 studies (971 patients) found:

  • Comparable efficacy for endogenous hyperpigmentation (RR 1.06)
  • Trend toward superiority for exogenous hyperpigmentation (RR 1.39, p=0.05)
  • Significantly less PIH with PSLs (p=0.02) and less PIH hypopigmentation (p=0.002)
  • Lower pain scores with PSLs (VAS 1.5–5.61 vs. 1.0–7.9, p<0.01) [11]

Safety for Darker Skin (Fitzpatrick IV–VI)

Multiple studies support safety of 1064 nm picosecond lasers for darker skin types:

  • Chan et al.: Fractional Nd:YAG PS laser in Fitzpatrick III–IV Asian women with facial melasma showed no hypo- or hyperpigmentation; described as "minimally painful" [10]
  • Kaewkes et al.: No cases of hypo- or hyperpigmentation in FST IV–V with 1064 nm fractional PS laser for striae [10]
  • NUBWAY clinical summary: Picosecond lasers use sound waves (not heat) to break up pigment, reducing PIH risk; longer wavelengths (1064 nm) recommended for darker skin [12]

However, one notable caution: the 2026 Cureus systematic review (52 RCTs) found overall pooled analysis showed non-significant improvement for laser-based therapies for melasma (MD 0.70, 95% CI -0.55 to 1.95, p=0.2682), with low certainty of evidence [13]. This suggests that while individual studies show promise, the overall evidence base requires higher-quality research.

3. Selective Photothermolysis Advancements

Fractional Photothermolysis

Fractional photothermolysis creates microscopic columns of thermal damage surrounded by untreated skin, enabling faster healing. Key developments include:

  • Non-ablative fractional diode laser (NFDL): Uses 1440 nm and 1927 nm wavelengths; water molecules absorb infrared energy, improving dyschromia in skin of color with reduced adverse effects compared to other fractional lasers [14].
  • Hybrid fractional lasers: Devices like the Alma Hybrid combine CO₂ (10,600 nm) with 1570 nm lasers for simultaneous ablative and non-ablative resurfacing in a single session [15].
  • Minimal photothermolysis: Garg et al. (2024) described using spot sizes and fluences below the selective photothermolysis threshold (<3 J/cm²) for treating dermal hyperpigmentation in skin of color, achieving clinical improvement with reduced risk [16].

Advanced Optical Delivery Systems

Modern picosecond lasers employ sophisticated fractional optical arrays:

  • Diffractive Lens Arrays (DLA): Used in 755 nm Picosure laser to concentrate peak energies within microbeams
  • Micro-Lens Arrays (MLA): Used in 532/1064 nm PICOPLUS for zones of high-intensity tissue damage with adjacent preservation
  • Holographic optical matrices: Additional platforms providing varying spot sizes for customized treatment [10]

Non-Invasive Treatments for Fitzpatrick IV–VI

The 2026 PMC review identified key safety strategies:

  • RF microneedling: Electrothermal effect avoids melanin targeting; starting at 15 kW, gradually increasing to max 40 kW for soft tissue [4]
  • Chemical peels: Pretreatment with hydroquinone 2% for ≥2 weeks before peels to protect against PIH [4]
  • Laser parameters: Longer wavelengths, lower fluences, shorter pulse widths, fewer passes, increased inter-treatment intervals [4]
  • Topical retinoids: Recommended pre-treatment to speed reepithelialization and decrease hyperpigmentation severity [4]

Safety Concerns for Darker Skin Tones (Fitzpatrick IV–VI)

General Risks

  • Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH): More prevalent in Fitzpatrick IV–VI; lasers pose increased PIH rates with repeated procedures (11–17%) [26]
  • Increased sensitivity: People with darker skin tones show up to 50% lower maximum tolerated dose for visible light LED therapy compared to lighter skins [14]
  • Higher temperatures: Doses of 8–12 J/cm² can cause significant thermal-induced pain in skin of color [14]
  • Melanoma misdiagnosis risk: Theoretical risk of inadvertently treating a melanoma that is misdiagnosed as a benign lesion; light therapy does not provide histologic evaluation, and residual melanocytes can remain [27]
  • Inadequate monitoring: Residual pigment after laser treatment makes clinical monitoring of recurrent lesions challenging [27]

Mitigation Strategies

  1. Use longer wavelengths (1064 nm for deeper targets; 675 nm for superficial melasma)
  2. Employ lower fluences and shorter pulse durations
  3. Pre-treat with hydroquinone 2% (2+ weeks) or topical retinoids
  4. Post-treat with sun protection, mask-wearing, and anti-inflammatory regimens
  5. For LED therapy, start with lower parameters and "scout" for adverse effects
  6. For RF: start at 15 kW and titrate upward gradually
  7. Perform test patches before full treatment
  8. Use pre- and post-treatment cryotherapy/cooling
  9. Selective photothermolysis with sub-threshold energy (<3 J/cm²) in minimal photothermolysis approach [16]

Unmet Gaps

  • Long-term safety data for light/laser therapies in Fitzpatrick VI is extremely limited
  • More research is needed on PBM in skin of color (existing evidence is "limited" per the 2024 review) [14]
  • The 2025 MDPI review on LIOB used 3D skin models; in vivo human data for diverse phototypes remains scarce [5]
  • Comparative effectiveness studies directly comparing newer technologies (picosecond, yellow LED) against established modalities in larger, ethnically diverse cohorts are needed

Light-Based Therapies in Aesthetic Medicine: Current Applications and Recent Scientific Advancements (2020–2026)

Light-based therapies encompass three principal technologies used in aesthetic dermatology: monochromatic lasers, polychromatic intense pulsed light (IPL), and non-coherent light-emitting diodes (LEDs) operating via photobiomodulation (PBM). Research published between 2020 and early 2026 demonstrates convergence on several themes: safer pigment clearance using longer wavelengths and lower energies for skin of color; hybrid multi-wavelength platforms delivering ablative and non-ablative effects simultaneously; and combination strategies pairing light devices with topical depigmenting agents rather than relying on monotherapy.

---

1. Laser Therapy

1.1 Photoaging Treatment

Fractional ablative lasers remain the gold standard for moderate-to-severe photoaging. Fractional CO₂ (10,600 nm) achieves approximately 70% reduction in wrinkles and ~85% improvement in overall skin quality [1], though downtime averages 7–14 days. Fractional Er:YAG (2,940 nm) delivers comparable efficacy with shorter healing (~3–7 days) due to minimal collateral thermal damage [1].

Non-ablative fractional lasers (Nd:YAG at 1,320 nm; diode lasers at 1,440/1,927 nm; thulium at 1,927 nm) stimulate collagen remodeling without epidermal disruption and typically require less than 48 hours of downtime [1][2].

1.2 Hyperpigmentation — Age Spots / Solar Lentigines

Picosecond Nd:YAG lasers have emerged as the preferred modality over traditional Q-switched nanosecond lasers:

  • 1064-nm picosecond Nd:YAG: Bernstein et al. demonstrated significant mMASI improvement at both 3 and 8 months post-treatment [3]. Chan et al. achieved a 75% total mMASI decrease at 6 weeks in Fitzpatrick III–IV patients with no hypo- or hyperpigmentation [3].
  • 532-nm picosecond Nd:YAG: Achieves 93.02% excellent-to-great improvement with significantly lower PIH (~5%) versus ~30% with conventional Q-switched Nd:YAG at 532 nm [4].
  • 730-nm picosecond laser: Achieves 78.4% clearance after two sessions with 90% patient satisfaction at 6-month follow-up [3].

The mechanism involves laser-induced optical breakdown (LIOB) — a photoacoustic rather than purely photothermal effect generated by ultra-short picosecond pulses that shatters melanin clusters into smaller particles with minimal thermal diffusion [3][5]. Histological studies using 3D melanocyte-containing skin models confirmed LIOB triggers dermal remodeling cascades including upregulation of MMPs (MMP-1, -3, -9), collagens (COL1A1, COL3A1), and heat shock proteins [5].

A 2025 meta-analysis (Wu et al., 21 studies, 971 patients) found picosecond lasers had significantly less PIH (p = 0.02), less PIH hypopigmentation (p = 0.002), lower pain scores (VAS p < 0.01), and comparable endogenous hyperpigmentation clearance versus nanosecond lasers (RR 1.06) [6].

1.3 Melasma & Skin Brightening

Melasma remains one of the most challenging indications due to high recurrence rates.

Low-fluence Q-Switched Nd:YAG (1064 nm): Remains the most established laser-based brightening approach. Weekly/biweekly treatments at 1–3 J/cm² using large spot sizes (6–10 mm) induce subthreshold photoacoustic/photo-mechanical disruption of melanosomes ("subcellular selective photothermolysis") without destroying melanocyte membranes [7]. Typically requires 5–15 sessions but carries high recurrence rates (64–81% at 3 months) [8].

Picosecond Nd:YAG (1064 nm): Significant improvements documented even in Fitzpatrick IV–V skin [3][9]. However, a 2026 systematic review (Cureus, 52 RCTs) found non-significant pooled improvement for laser-based therapies for melasma overall (MD 0.70, 95% CI −0.55 to 1.95, p = 0.2682), underscoring limitations of existing evidence [10].

Critical caveat: Laser monotherapy may worsen melasma or cause rebound lesions after discontinuation [8]. Combination therapy with topical agents is strongly preferred [8].

1.4 Hybrid Fractional Lasers (Key Recent Advance)

The Sciton Halo system delivers coincident fractional Er:YAG (2,940 nm) and non-ablative 1,470 nm infrared simultaneously — ablative pigment clearance combined with deep collagen stimulation — achieving approximately 80% improvement with median downtime of only 3 days [11].

The concept of minimal photothermolysis (Garg et al., 2024) uses spot sizes and fluences below the conventional threshold (<3 J/cm²) for treating dermal hyperpigmentation in skin of color, achieving clinical improvement with reduced risk [12].

2. Intense Pulsed Light (IPL)

2.1 Technology Fundamentals

IPL emits non-coherent polychromatic light (typically 500–1200 nm), enabling simultaneous targeting of melanin, hemoglobin, and water through interchangeable filters [13].

2.2 Current Platforms

Lumenis Stellar M22 with XPL™ Technology (launched November 2025): Features 8 expert filters, sub-pulsing technology, Advanced Pulse Design, Glide Mode for faster treatments, and 850+ built-in protocols. Clinical data showed ≥70% improvement in a single session with 76–100% clearance of vascular/pigmented lesions [14].

InMode Lumecca IPL: Delivers up to 3× more energy in the 500–600 nm range with peak power of 3.3 kW/cm². Available in variants optimized for lighter (515–1200 nm) or darker skin types (580–1200 nm), achieving results in 1–3 sessions versus 4–6 for traditional IPL [15].

2.3 Solar Lentigines (Age Spots)

IPL achieves success rates of 74.6–94.4% depending on parameters and anatomical location [4], generally offering the best balance of efficacy and safety for lentigines compared to single-wavelength lasers [4].

2.4 Melasma

IPL works better for epidermal melasma than dermal deposits [16][17]. A 2024 Pakistani study (n = 62) comparing IPL (560 nm filter) versus intradermal tranexamic acid found IPL achieved 57.1% mean mMASI reduction vs. 42.2% for TXA (p = 0.0034), with milder side effects [18]. Outcomes converged at 3 months (p = 0.29), suggesting IPL provides faster initial improvement but maintenance requires adjunctive therapy [18].

Guidelines strongly recommend combining IPL with topical lightening agents for optimal outcomes [8].

3. LED Photobiomodulation (PBM)

3.1 Mechanism of Action

LED therapy operates through absorption by mitochondrial cytochrome c oxidase, leading to increased ATP production, TGF-β/SMAD pathway activation (stimulating procollagen expression), MMP downregulation, and tyrosinase/MITF modulation [19][20].

3.2 Key Wavelengths and Evidence

Yellow/Amber LEDs (570–590 nm):

  • Yi et al. (2025, RCT, n = 30): Yellow light combination was superior to red/infrared-only LED for reducing brown spots and improving wrinkles [21].
  • Amber/yellow LED has been identified as effective for reducing melanin content through tyrosinase downregulation [22].

Red LEDs (630 ± 10 nm):

  • Lee et al. (2007, RCT, n = 76): Landmark trial showing 26–36% significant wrinkle reduction with histological confirmation of increased collagen and elastic fibers adjacent to highly active fibroblasts [19].

Near-Infrared LEDs (830–850 nm):

  • Kim et al. (2012): Irradiation decreased melanin synthesis without cytotoxic effects in melanocyte cultures through tyrosinase, TRP-1, and MITF downregulation [20].

3.3 Professional vs. Home Devices

Professional devices deliver higher power densities (30–100 mW/cm²); home-use masks operate at lower intensities (~10 mW/cm² max). The JAAD 2025 consensus confirmed red light PBM does not induce DNA damage even at fluences up to 1280 J/cm² [23].

4. Combination Approaches and Synergistic Technologies

4.1 Laser + Topical Agents

Combination of light devices with tranexamic acid, niacinamide, or other depigmenting agents shows consistent superiority over monotherapy. A 2024 RCT found picosecond laser + TXA via microchannels (from fractional mode) produced greater melasma lightening than either modality alone, attributed to enhanced trans-epidermal drug delivery [24]. A 2026 meta-analysis (11 RCTs) found combination therapy with topical agents showed statistically significant advantage at 8 weeks (SMD: −0.58, p = 0.004) [25].

4.2 Radiofrequency (RF) + Light Hybrids

RF microneedling is inherently safe for all Fitzpatrick types because its electrothermal mechanism targets interstitial fluid, not melanin [2][26]. The Lumenis Polaris WRA (diode 900 nm + bipolar RF) showed greater dermal collagen improvement in a split-face design (64.3% good/excellent) [27]. A combined needle RF + IPL device for acne showed 34.8% mean lesion reduction at 12 weeks (p < 0.05) [28].

4.3 675-nm Laser

A 2025 study in 28 Thai patients (85.71% FST IV) using 675-nm monotherapy (RedTouch®) showed 41% mMASI reduction at 3 months (p < 0.0001) with no PIH, only mild transient erythema, and no serious adverse events [29]. This represents a promising option for darker skin types.

5. Safety for Darker Skin Tones (Fitzpatrick IV–VI)

Key strategies include: use of longer wavelengths (1064 nm, 675 nm); sub-threshold energy (minimal photothermolysis, <3 J/cm²); pre-treatment with hydroquinone 2% (≥2 weeks) or retinoids; and test patches before full treatment [2][12]. The 1,064-nm picosecond laser and 675-nm 675-nm laser are the most supported options for FST IV–V, with no reported cases of hypo- or hyperpigmentation in available case series [3][29][30].

---

6. Limitations

  • No large RCTs exist for LED monotherapy for melasma as a primary endpoint [20][22].
  • Long-term data (>1 year) for picosection lasers in melasma management remains sparse [7].
  • The 2026 Cureus meta-analysis found non-significant overall improvement for laser-based melasma therapies, with low certainty of evidence [10].
  • Underpowered melasma trials (e.g., the Galache 2025 pilot RCT for amber LED vs. TXA was 51% powered) [22].
  • Standardized treatment parameters across the field remain an unmet need.
