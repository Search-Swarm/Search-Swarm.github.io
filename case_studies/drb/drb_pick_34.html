Horizontal Gene Transfer (HGT) in Plants and Animals: A Comprehensive Analysis

Executive Summary

Horizontal gene transfer—the movement of genetic material between organisms that are not in a parent-offspring relationship—was once considered a phenomenon exclusive to prokaryotes. However, genomic advances over the past two decades have revealed that HGT occurs across all domains of life, including multicellular plants and animals. While far less frequent than in bacteria (~81% of bacterial genes show evidence of past HGT [1]), HGT in eukaryotes remains a genuine evolutionary force that has shaped major biological transitions and conferred adaptive advantages.

---

1. Prevalence and Mechanisms of HGT in Plants

Key Documented Cases

Parasitic plant-to-host transfers: The first documented plant HGT involved Cuscuta (dodder) transferring mitochondrial genes (atp1, atp6, matR) to host Plantago species [2]. Dodder subsequently acquired 108 functional nuclear genes from host plants, contributing to haustoria development, defense responses, and amino acid metabolism [3].

Host-to-parasite nuclear gene transfer: Yoshida et al. (2010) demonstrated that the parasitic weed Striga hermonthica captured nuclear genes from its grass hosts (e.g., sorghum), representing the first evidence of nuclear gene capture by parasitic weeds from hosts in nature [4].

Agrobacterium T-DNA transfer: At least 93 naturally transgenic plant species carry integrated Agrobacterium-derived T-DNA sequences, with integration events spanning 0.62–24 million years ago [5][6].

Gymnosperm-to-angiosperm transfer: Won & Renner (2003) discovered mitochondrial nad1 intron/exon transfer from an asterid to Gnetum (gymnosperms). A 2024 study found 36,635–194,613 bp of foreign angiosperm DNA in Asian Gnetum mitogenomes from multiple independent HGT events >10 million years ago [7][8].

Land colonization PAL gene: Emiliani et al. (2009) demonstrated through phylogenetic analysis that the ancestor of land plants acquired phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL) via HGT from soil bacteria, likely mediated through arbuscular mycorrhizal symbioses. This enzyme catalyzes the first step of the phenylpropanoid pathway, which produces flavonoids (for UV protection) and lignin (for structural support)—both essential for terrestrial life. The paper states: "The horizontally acquired PAL represented then the basis for further development of the phenylpropanoid pathway and plant radiation on terrestrial environments" [9].

C4 photosynthesis optimization: In the grass Alloteropsis semialata, 59 functional genes from at least nine donor species were transferred in 23 large DNA fragments (up to 170 kb), including photosynthesis-related genes. These transfers contributed to optimization of C4 photosynthesis over the last ~10 million years [10][11].

Fhb7 disease resistance gene: Wang et al. (2020) demonstrated that the Fhb7 glutathione S-transferase gene in wheat relative Thinopyrum elongatum originated from the endophytic fungus Epichloë. This gene enables enzymatic detoxification of trichothecene mycotoxins produced by Fusarium pathogens, conferring resistance to Fusarium head blight [12].

Neochrome gene (hornworts → ferns): Li et al. (2014) showed that the chimeric photoreceptor gene neochrome was transferred from hornworts to ferns via HGT ~179 million years ago. This adaptation enables ferns to sense both red and blue light under low-light forest understory conditions, contributing to fern diversification [13].

Transposable element transfers: El Baidouri et al. found 32 horizontal transfers of transposable elements among 40 angiosperm species, extrapolating that "hundreds of thousands of HTTs had occurred among flowering plants within the last two million years" [3].

---

2. Prevalent Mechanisms Facilitating Plant HGT

  • Parasitism/haustoria: Direct vascular connections through haustoria enable physical transfer of DNA, proteins, mRNAs, and organelles [3]
  • Grafting: Vascular tissue fusion between grafted species can result in complete organelle (chloroplast) and nuclear genome transfer
  • Endosymbiont relationships (Wolbachia, Agrobacterium)
  • Intergenomic gene flow through food chains

4. Why Is HGT So Rare in Eukaryotes?

Multiple stacked barriers prevent HGT in eukaryotes:

Nuclear envelope: Foreign DNA must cross the double-membrane nuclear envelope via the nuclear pore complex. DNA >9 nm cannot passively diffuse through NPC [25].

Germline segregation: In animals, somatic cells are physically separated from germ cells. Only germline mutations are heritable. Foreign DNA in somatic cells is evolutionary dead weight [26].

RNA interference/silencing: Plants use RNA-directed DNA methylation (RdDM); animals use piRNA pathways in the germline. Both systems target and silence foreign nucleic acids as a natural immune defense [27][28].

Complexity hypothesis: Eukaryotic genes participate in more complex protein-protein interaction networks. A foreign gene that cannot properly integrate into these networks is selected against. Burch et al. (2023) confirmed that the ability of a transferred gene to engage in normal protein-protein interactions is a key determinant of HGT success [29].

Multicellularity: Each additional step—entry, nuclear import, integration, maintenance through division, transmission to offspring—represents a potential failure point [30].

No natural transformation machinery: Unlike bacteria, eukaryotes lack constitutive mechanisms for DNA uptake and recombination [31].

---

5. Evolutionary Significance of Adaptive HGT

Despite its rarity, when HGT occurs in eukaryotes, it often delivers substantial adaptive benefits:

  1. Major ecological transitions: PAL acquisition enabled land colonization by plants [9]; neochrome acquisition enabled ferns to thrive in low-light environments [13].
  2. Metabolic innovation: C4 photosynthesis optimization through repeated gene transfers [10]; novel detoxification pathways (trichothecene GST in wheat; cyanide detoxification in mites; mannanase in coffee borers) [12][20][21].
  3. Herbivory adaptation: Cell wall-degrading enzymes in nematodes; carotenoid biosynthesis in aphids; phenolic glycoside detoxification in whiteflies [18][19][32].
  4. Defense innovation: Aerolysin pore-forming toxins in ticks; antibiotic biosynthetic clusters in bdelloid rotifers [24][33].
  5. Symbiosis establishment: Wolbachia-derived metabolic genes provisioning insect hosts [17].

---

6. Remaining Uncertainties

  • The precise mechanisms facilitating many non-obvious plant-to-plant HGT events remain unclear [3].
  • Whether many transferred genes are truly functional versus selectively neutral is difficult to assess [3].
  • The debate over HGT in the human genome continues, with methodological challenges in distinguishing true HGT from differential gene loss [34].
  • In bdelloid rotifers, the mechanism by which foreign DNA enters the genome (given their obligate asexuality) remains poorly understood [22].
  • Some researchers question whether some candidate HGT regions represent genuine transfers versus incomplete lineage sorting [34].

---

Conclusion

HGT in plants and animals is rare but consequential. It operates through specific biological interfaces (haustoria, endosymbioses, asexual reproduction) that bypass eukaryotic barriers. When successful, HGT delivers genes that confer immediate selective advantages—detoxification capabilities, metabolic innovations, defense mechanisms, and ecological niche expansion. The most dramatic examples include the acquisition of PAL from bacteria enabling plant colonization of land, and the acquisition of carotenoid biosynthesis genes from fungi enabling aphids to synthesize their own pigments. Far from being evolutionarily insignificant, HGT in eukaryotes represents a genuine alternative pathway for adaptive innovation that has shaped the history of life on Earth.

Horizontal Gene Transfer (HGT) in Plants: Comprehensive Review of Well-Documented Cases

Horizontal gene transfer—the non-sexual movement of genetic material between organisms—is far more prevalent in plants than initially appreciated. Recent genomic studies have revealed hundreds to potentially hundreds of thousands of HGT events across plant lineages. Below is a synthesis of the best-documented cases, organized by donor–recipient relationship.

---

1. Parasitic Plant-to-Host HGT (Plant × Plant)

1a. Dodder (Cuscuta) → Host Plants (Plantago, Bartsia)

The first documented case of HGT in plants involved the parasitic genus Cuscuta (dodder) transferring mitochondrial genes to its hosts. Mower et al. (2001) reported that the mitochondrial atp1 gene was transferred from Cuscuta and Bartsia into Plantago species [1]. This was followed by a landmark study showing that three mitochondrial genes—atp1, atp6, and matR—were transferred together from Cuscuta to Plantago via a single DNA-mediated event [2]. The foreign genes became pseudogenes shortly after transfer and are not transcribed, but gene conversion between native and foreign copies of atp1 was detected, suggesting ongoing evolutionary interaction [2].

1b. Dodder Stealing >100 Functional Genes from Hosts

A major study led by Penn State researchers identified 108 functional genes that dodder (Cuscuta campestris and related species) acquired from host plants through HGT [3]. These genes contribute to haustoria structure, defense responses, amino acid metabolism, and even produce microRNAs that may silence host defense genes. Eighteen of these genes are conserved across all dodder species, indicating ancient transfers [3]. Additionally, 42 non-functional transferred regions were identified [3].

1c. Orobanchaceae: Striga, Phelipanche, Orobanche

Yang et al. (2016) identified 52 high-confidence HGT events in four parasitic Orobanchaceae species—Striga hermonthica, Phelipanche aegyptiaca, Orobanche aegyptiaca, and Triphysaria versicolor—using genome-scale transcriptomic data [4]. Key findings include:

  • Direction: Predominantly host-to-parasite transfer, with frequency increasing with parasitic dependency [4].
  • Donor specificity: All HGTs from grass donors (Poaceae) were found in Striga, while Phelipanche received genes from Rosaceae and Fabaceae hosts [4].
  • Functional categories: Defense-related genes (cytochrome P450, receptor-like kinases), translation factors, and transposable elements [4].
  • Expression: Many HGT genes are expressed in haustorial tissues and evolve under purifying selection, suggesting adaptive significance [4].
  • Scale: Kado & Innan (2018) estimated that 0.1–0.2% of obligate parasitic plant genes originated from HGT [1].

1d. Multi-step Transfer Chain: Cuscuta → Genisteae → Orobanche

A 2025 study documented a remarkable three-step transfer chain: a plastid gene fragment ("Pytheas sequence") from Cuscuta underwent intracellular gene transfer to its mitochondrial genome, then horizontally transferred to a Fabaceae host (tribe Genisteae), and finally to the holoparasite Orobanche rigens [5]. This represents the first comprehensively documented multi-step pathway linking three plant species through one intracellular transfer and two horizontal transfers [5].

1e. Host-to-Parasite Mitochondrial Gene Transfer in Orobanchaceae

A study on Orobanche coerulescens provided the first evidence of host-to-parasite mitochondrial gene transfer within Orobanchaceae, showing that the atp6 gene was replaced by a copy acquired from its Asteraceae host (Artemisia) [6].

2. Host-to-Parasite Nuclear Gene Transfer (Striga → Sorghum/crops)

Yoshida et al. (2010): First Nuclear Gene Transfer from Host to Parasite

Yoshida et al. demonstrated that a nuclear gene widely conserved among grass species—but absent in other eudicots—was present in the genome of Striga hermonthica (witchweed), clustering phylogenetically with sorghum genes [7]. This was the first evidence that nuclear genes can be captured by parasitic weeds from their hosts in nature, suggesting mRNA or cDNA intermediates may mediate such transfers [7][8].

---

3. Agrobacterium T-DNA Transfer to Plants

Natural Transformants

Agrobacterium causes crown gall disease by transferring T-DNA from its tumor-inducing (Ti) plasmid into plant genomes. This process has been co-opted as the primary tool for plant genetic engineering.

Nicotiana glauca was discovered in 1983 to carry Agrobacterium rhizogenes-like sequences in its nuclear genome, termed cellular T-DNAs (cT-DNAs) [9]. The cT-DNA contains several functional genes: rolB, rolC, orf13, orf14, and mikimopine synthase (mis) [9]. A 1986 study proposed that this natural transformation occurred early in the evolution of the genus Nicotiana [10]. More recent work has identified at least 93 naturally transgenic plant species, with Agrobacterium-to-plant transfer events estimated to have occurred between 0.62 and 24 million years ago [11].

Sweet potato (Ipomoea batatas) also carries naturally integrated T-DNA sequences from Agrobacterium, including acs (agrocinopine synthase), C-prot, IaaH, IaaM, RolB, and ORF18 [1].

Mechanism

The T-DNA (~8–11 kb) is processed by VirD2 endonuclease at 25-bp border sequences, transferred via a Type IV secretion system (VirB proteins), and guided into the nucleus by a nuclear localization signal on VirD2 [12]. Any DNA inserted between border sequences can be transferred, making Agrobacterium the premier vector for plant biotechnology [12].

4. Gymnosperm-to-Angiosperm HGT

Won & Renner (2003): Gnetum

Won and Renner discovered that the mitochondrial nad1 intron 2 and adjacent exons b and c were horizontally transferred from an asterid (likely Solanaceae) to Gnetum (Gnetales, gymnosperms) [13]. This was a landmark finding because it represented HGT between distantly related angiosperms and gymnosperms.

Pinus canariensis (2015)

Wang et al. reported a new case in which a mitochondrial nad5-1 fragment was transferred from an unknown angiosperm (likely a eudicot) to the Canary Island pine (Pinus canariensis) [14]. The foreign copy is located in the mitochondrial genome but is nonfunctional (pseudogenized). It became fixed in P. canariensis likely through genetic drift during colonization of the Canary Islands in the late Miocene [14].

Massive Angiosperm DNA Integration into Asian Gnetum (2024)

A recent study found enormous quantities of foreign angiosperm DNA in Asian Gnetum mitogenomes: 36,635 to 194,613 bp per species (6.4–14.2% of total mitogenome size) [15]. Donors include mostly eudicots from the Rubiaceae genus Coptosapelta (contributing 11 foreign genes) and ten genera of Malpighiales [15]. Multiple independent HGT events occurred before the split of Asian Gnetum clades approximately 10–39 million years ago [15].

5. Photosynthesis-Related Gene Transfers

Neochrome Gene: Hornworts → Ferns (Li et al. 2014)

The chimeric photoreceptor gene neochrome—a fusion of phytochrome and phototropin domains—was transferred from hornworts (a bryophyte lineage) to ferns via HGT [16]. Neochrome enables ferns to sense both red and blue light, promoting chloroplast movement under low-light conditions [16]. This adaptation allowed ferns to thrive in shaded forest understories and is believed to have contributed to the diversification of modern ferns [16][17].

PEPC/PPC Gene in C4 Photosynthesis

Phosphoenolpyruvate carboxylase (PEPC) is a key enzyme in C4 photosynthesis. Christin et al. (2012) demonstrated that fundamental elements of the C4 pathway in the grass lineage Alloteropsis were acquired via at least four independent lateral gene transfers from C4 taxa that diverged more than 20 million years ago [18]. These transfers occurred repeatedly over at least the last 10 million years and contributed to optimization of the C4 pathway [18].

Alloteropsis semialata: Grass-to-Grass Gene Transfer

In the grass Alloteropsis semialata, 59 functional genes were acquired from at least nine different donor species as part of 23 large fragments of foreign DNA (up to 170 kb), including genes for photosynthesis, disease resistance, and stress tolerance [19][20]. This demonstrates that plant-to-plant lateral gene transfer can act as a potent source of genetic novelty and adaptation in flowering plants [18].

6. Fungus-to-Plant HGT

Fhb7 Gene: Epichloë → Wheat Relative (Thinopyrum elongatum)

Wang et al. (2020) cloned the Fhb7 gene from the wheat relative Thinopyrum elongatum, which confers resistance to Fusarium head blight (FHB)—a devastating fungal disease of wheat [21]. The gene shows little homology to plant genes but high homology to fungal genes. Phylogenetic analysis indicates it originated from the endophytic fungus Epichloë [21]. Fhb7 encodes a glutathione S-transferase (GST) that enzymatically detoxifies trichothecene mycotoxins produced by Fusarium pathogens [21]. Introduction of Fhb7 into diverse wheat cultivars confers resistance without yield penalty [21], offering a major breakthrough for wheat breeding.

7. Ancient HGT in Early Land Plant Evolution

Yue et al. (2012): Moss (Physcomitrella patens)

Genome analyses of the moss Physcomitrella patens identified 57 families of nuclear genes acquired from prokaryotes, fungi, and viruses [22]. Of these, 39 families appear to have been acquired during or after land plant evolution. Key acquired gene families include:

  • Subtilases from bacteria (involved in xylem differentiation, cuticle development, stomatal formation)
  • Arginase from bacteria (polyamine biosynthesis, stress tolerance)
  • Acyl-activating enzyme 18 (AAE18) and YUCCA flavin monooxygenase (YUC3) from bacteria (auxin biosynthesis)
  • Glutamate-cysteine ligase (GCL) from bacteria (glutathione biosynthesis, defense)
  • Nitrogen recycling genes (guanine deaminase, allantoate amidohydrolase, ureidoglycolate amidohydrolase)
  • Starch biosynthesis genes

These findings suggest that HGT played a critical role in the transition of plants from aquatic to terrestrial environments [22].

8. Massive Mitochondrial Genome HGT

Amborella trichopoda

The basal angiosperm Amborella trichopoda possesses a massive 3.9-megabase mitochondrial genome containing six genome equivalents of foreign mitochondrial DNA, acquired from green algae, mosses, and other angiosperms [23]. Many transfers were large, including acquisition of entire mitochondrial genomes from three green algae and one moss [23]. Bergthorsson et al. (2004) showed that Amborella acquired at least 20 of its 31 known mitochondrial protein genes from other land plants, with no evidence of HGT in any of the five sequenced angiosperm mitochondrial genomes for comparison [24]. This represents proportionately more HGT than any other eukaryotic or prokaryotic genome examined [24].

9. Cross-Kingdom Transfers

Animal-to-Plant: Insect Transferrin Genes → Cacao

Bai et al. reported transfer of two Transferrin genes from insects to Theobroma cacao (cacao tree) [1].

Plant-to-Animal: BtPMaT1 Gene → Whitefly

Xia et al. (2021) demonstrated that the sweet potato whitefly (Bemisia tabaci) acquired the plant-derived gene BtPMaT1, encoding a phenolic glycoside malonyltransferase, through HGT [25]. This gene enables the whitefly to detoxify plant phenolic glycosides during feeding, representing a remarkable example of a plant defense gene being co-opted by an insect herbivore [25].

Remaining Uncertainties

  • The precise mechanisms facilitating most plant-to-plant HGT events remain unclear, though haustorial connections in parasitic plants are clearly implicated.
  • Whether many transferred genes are truly functional versus selectively neutral remains difficult to assess; most functional validation studies focus on specific candidate genes.
  • The true scale of HGT in plants may be vastly underestimated, with recent estimates suggesting hundreds of thousands of transposable element transfers among flowering plants alone [1].
  • Some cases (particularly ancient ones in mitochondrial genomes) cannot be independently validated beyond phylogenetic evidence.

Well-Documented Cases of Horizontal Gene Transfer (HGT) in Animals


Overview


Horizontal gene transfer (HGT), once considered primarily a prokaryotic phenomenon, is now recognized as an important evolutionary mechanism across all domains of life. In animals, HGT events are relatively rare compared to bacteria but have been robustly documented and often confer significant adaptive advantages. Below is a comprehensive survey organized by the categories specified.


---


1. HGT from Endosymbionts (Wolbachia) to Animal Genomes


Wolbachia, an intracellular α-proteobacterium infecting an estimated 40% of insect species, is the most prolific source of HGT to animal nuclear genomes. The intimate association between Wolbachia and host germline cells provides a direct route for DNA transfer that can be inherited vertically once established.


1.1 Callosobruchus chinensis (Adzuki Bean Beetle) — First Discovery


The first robust case of Wolbachia-to-insect HGT was reported in 2002 in the adzuki bean beetle Callosobruchus chinensis (Kondo et al. 2002, PNAS). Using PCR screening of 205 Wolbachia genes, researchers found that approximately 30% (~380 kb, ~360 genes) of the Wolbachia genome had been integrated into the insect nuclear genome. Fluorescent in situ hybridization (FISH) localized the transferred genes to the proximal region of the short arm of the X chromosome [1][2].


1.2 Aedes aegypti (Mosquito) — Two Adjacent Genes


Klasson et al. (2009) identified a case of HGT involving two adjacent genes (AAEL004181 and AAEL004188) between Wolbachia and the mosquito Aedes aegypti. The genes share ~50% amino acid identity with Wolbachia wPip genes, lack introns (typical of bacterial genes), and are transcribed in both male and female adult mosquitoes. Homologs were also found in Ae. mascarensis, suggesting the transfer predates speciation. The genes are located within Wolbachia prophage regions, providing a mechanistic explanation for their transfer [3].


1.3 Drosophila ananassae — Entire Wolbachia Genome Integration


The largest known Wolbachia-to-host HGT event involves the integration of nearly the entire ~1.4 Mbp Wolbachia genome into the nuclear genome of Drosophila ananassae, localized to chromosome 4 (Muller element F) [4][5]. At least 28 genes of Wolbachia origin are transcribed at low levels [2]. However, functional analysis revealed extensive pseudogenization: the integrated genome accumulated high proportions of nonsense and frameshift mutations, suggesting most transferred sequences are non-functional remnants [5].


1.4 Formica exsecta (Wood Ant) — Multiple Functional Genes


Dhaygude et al. (2019) identified five Wolbachia-derived scaffolds (13–47 kb each, plus one small fragment) containing 83 putative functional protein-coding genes in the ant Formica exsecta. These include ABC transporters, ankyrin repeat proteins, DNA repair proteins (RadC), carboxypeptidases, and methylases. At least 6 of these genes are transcribed at low levels. Critically, similar HGT events occurred independently in multiple other insect lineages including Vollenhovia emeryi, Cardiocondyla obscurior, Brugia pahangi (nematode), and parasitoid wasps, indicating parallel Wolbachia-to-host transfers are widespread [6].


1.5 Mechanism: Bacteriophage WO


Bacterial phage WO, which integrates into Wolbachia genomes, has been shown to mediate HGT between Wolbachia strains and even between Wolbachia and unrelated bacteria. Three validated transfer events include: (1) spvB gene carried by phage WOcauB3 between wCauB and wNo; (2) two ankyrin-repeat genes carried by phage WOcauB2 between wCauB and wRi; and (3) an entire prophage plus flanking bacterial DNA transferred by phage WOVitA1 [7].


1.6 Wolbachia-to-Nematode HGT


In filarial nematodes like Brugia malayi, ~249 contigs containing Wolbachia sequence fragments were identified. Even in Wolbachia-free lineages (Acanthocheilonema viteae, Onchocerca flexuosa), numerous Wolbachia-ancestry genes were confirmed by whole-genome sequencing, with tissue-specific transcription observed [2].

2. HGT from Fungi to Animals


2.1 Aphid Carotenoid Biosynthesis Genes (Moran et al. 2010)


This is perhaps the most iconic example of cross-kingdom HGT in animals. Pea aphids (Acyrthosiphon pisum) possess multiple enzymes for carotenoid biosynthesis whose phylogenetic analyses place them firmly within fungal sequences, not among any known animal genes [8]. A striking 30-kilobase genomic region encoding a carotenoid desaturase is present in red-colored individuals but absent from green individuals. A mutation in this desaturase gene causes loss of torulene and red body color. This demonstrates that aphids are animals capable of de novo carotenoid production through horizontal gene transfer from fungi — carotenoids that are otherwise produced only by plants, fungi, and microorganisms [8].


2.2 Spider Mite Cyanide Detoxification (Navarrete-Sombra et al. 2014)


The two-spotted spider mite (Tetranychus urticae) possesses a β-cyanoalanine synthase (CAS) gene with bacterial origin (notably from cysteine synthase family), acquired via HGT [9]. Recombinant enzyme assays confirmed strong cyanide detoxification activity. Genomic evidence (synteny with eukaryotic genes, codon amelioration, absence of introns) confirmed integration into the mite genome rather than bacterial contamination. Homologous genes have also been identified in Lepidoptera, enabling multiple insect lineages to feed on cyanogenic plants [9].


---


3. HGT in Nematodes


3.1 Plant Cell Wall-Degrading Enzymes in Plant-Parasitic Nematodes


Plant-parasitic nematodes (PPN) represent a remarkable case where HGT from bacteria enabled the evolution of plant parasitism. Scholl et al. (2003) identified genes in root-knot nematodes (Meloidogyne incognita) with bacterial origin encoding cellulases, pectate lyases, and other cell wall-degrading enzymes [10]. Danchin et al. (2010) showed that independent lateral gene transfers from different bacteria provided PPN with an arsenal of cell wall-degrading enzymes, while Danchin et al. (2016) demonstrated that the GH32 invertase genes from the potato cyst nematode Globodera pallida are functional bacterial invertases expressed during feeding on host-derived sucrose [11].


3.2 Cyanase Enzyme from Plants and Bacteria


Zarlenga et al. (2019) demonstrated that over 35 nematode species independently acquired the cyanase enzyme — typically found only in plants, bacteria, and fungi — via HGT from different kingdoms [12]. Clade I nematodes (e.g., Trichinella spiralis, Trichuris) acquired plant-derived cyanases, while clade III/IV nematodes (e.g., filarioids, ascaridoids) acquired bacterial cyanases. Western blots confirmed the enzyme is bioactive in T. spiralis, with immunohistochemistry localizing it to the hypodermis and muscle. The plant-derived acquisition likely occurred >400 million years ago, prior to the divergence of Trichinellida and Dioctophymatida [12].

4. HGT in Bdelloid Rotifers


Bdelloid rotifers hold the record for the highest levels of HGT in animals, with studies estimating that 8–10% of their genes originate from non-metazoan sources.


4.1 Gladyshev et al. (2008) — Discovery of Massive HGT


The landmark study by Gladyshev, Meselson, and Arkhipova (2008, Science) reported that bdelloid rotifers harbor many genes originating from bacteria, fungi, and plants, concentrated in telomeric regions alongside diverse mobile genetic elements [13]. Some foreign genes were intact and transcribed; some contained functional spliceosomal introns. One bacterial-origin gene was overexpressed in E. coli and yielded an active enzyme. The authors proposed that capture and functional assimilation of exogenous genes may be an important force in bdelloid evolution [13].


4.2 Boschetti et al. (2012) — Biochemical Diversification


Boschetti et al. (2012) demonstrated that hundreds of horizontally acquired genes contribute to biochemical diversification in the bdelloid rotifer Adineta ricciae, including cellulolytic enzymes from fungal and prokaryotic donors [14].


4.3 Nowell et al. (2024) — Defense Against Fungal Pathogens


Nowell et al. (2024) showed that bdelloid rotifers deploy horizontally acquired biosynthetic genes against the fungal pathogen Rosellinia globospora. When challenged with the pathogen, horizontally acquired genes were over twice as likely to be upregulated compared to other genes. The most markedly enriched gene clusters resemble bacterial polyketide synthetases (PKS) and nonribosomal peptide synthetases (NRP), which produce antibiotics. The resistant species A. ricciae upregulated three times more NRP/PKS clusters than the susceptible A. vaga, with fold-changes exceeding those seen for abiotic stress responses [15].


4.4 Eyres et al. (2015) — Ancient and Ongoing HGT


Eyres et al. (2015) showed that ~half of foreign genes in bdelloids were acquired before the divergence of bdelloid families over 60 million years ago, while HGT continues to occur in extant species. Desiccating habitats appear to facilitate greater gene acquisition [16].

5. HGT in Insects (Additional Cases)


5.1 Coffee Berry Borer — Bacterial Mannanase (HhMAN1)


The coffee berry borer beetle (Hypothenemus hampei) possesses the HhMAN1 gene, encoding a mannanase enzyme not previously found in insects [17]. Phylogenetic analysis places HhMAN1 firmly within bacterial (Bacillus) mannanases. The gene is integrated into the genome, flanked by eukaryotic transposable elements. It is present in all global populations tested but absent from closely related non-coffee-feeding species. Recombinant protein hydrolyzes coffee berry galactomannan, confirming functional activity. This adaptation likely enabled the beetle's specialization on coffee as a host plant [17].


5.2 oskar Gene — Origin of Germ Cell Specification in Holometabola


The oskar gene, essential for germ cell specification in holometabolous insects, originated through HGT from bacteria followed by domain fusion [18]. Analysis of over 100 insect Oskar sequences revealed that its N-terminal LOTUS domain is of eukaryotic origin, while its C-terminal OSK domain is nested within bacterial GDSL-like hydrolase sequences. Topology constraint tests confirmed that the closest relatives of the OSK domain are bacterial rather than eukaryotic. The fusion likely occurred before the major diversification of insects (~500 MYA). This represents a case where HGT generated an entirely novel gene with a new developmental function [18].


5.3 Psyllids — Carsonella Endosymbiont Compensation


The psyllid Pachypsylla venusta has undergone extensive HGT from its endosymbiont Carsonella ruddii (the smallest known bacterial genome). Host genes upregulated in bacteriocytes compensate for gene losses in the degenerating endosymbiont, facilitating extreme symbiont genome reduction [19].

6. HGT from Plants to Herbivorous Arthropods


6.1 Wybouw et al. (2016) — Comprehensive Study of Plant-to-Insect HGT


Wybouw et al. (2016) demonstrated that HGT has played a prominent role in arthropod herbivory adaptation. Horizontally transferred genes underpin adaptations including: (1) plant cell wall degradation (polygalacturonases, cellulases); (2) intracellular carbohydrate assimilation (UDP-glycosyltransferases); and (3) detoxification of plant defensive compounds [20]. These genes were identified across diverse phytophagous arthropods including chelicerates (mites) and hexapods (insects).


6.2 Leaf Beetles — Digestive Enzymes for Herbivory


More recent work has shown that horizontal gene transfers drive dietary specialization in leaf beetles. Metabolic enhancement through HGT has been identified as critical for the dietary specialization of certain leaf beetle species [21].

7. HGT in Primates/Humans


7.1 Crisp et al. (2015) — Expression of Multiple Foreign Genes


Crisp et al. (2015) analyzed HGT across 26 animal species including 10 primates and found that primates carry substantial numbers of horizontally acquired genes [22]. Humans were identified as having 145 class C foreign genes (with 110 class B and 39 class A), originating from bacteria, fungi, and plants. Key validated examples include:


  • Hyaluronan synthases (HAS1-3): Reclaimed as fungus-to-vertebrate HGT
  • FTO gene: Confirmed as class A HGT
  • ABO blood group gene: Confirmed as class A HGT


Functional enrichment analysis showed that 42% of enriched GO terms for foreign genes describe enzyme activities, compared to only 4% for native chordate genes lost in outgroups — ruling out gene-loss explanations [22].


7.2 Subsequent Debate


This finding has been debated. Huang et al. (2017) identified 1,467 conserved regions in the human genome more similar to non-mammalian vertebrates than mammals, enriched for ion-binding functions [23]. While some overlap with known HGT regions exists, most predicted HGT regions did not overlap with previously identified candidates, suggesting HGT may be more common than previously thought [23]. Other researchers have questioned whether some candidate genes reflect genuine HGT versus differential gene loss combined with varying evolutionary rates [24].

Remaining Uncertainties


  • The functional significance of many Wolbachia-derived genes in hosts remains unclear; most appear to be pseudogenized.
  • The debate over HGT in the human genome continues, with methodological challenges in distinguishing true HGT from incomplete lineage sorting or gene loss.
  • In bdelloid rotifers, the mechanism by which foreign DNA enters the genome (given their obligate asexuality) remains poorly understood.
  • Many HGT events appear difficult to date precisely, especially ancient ones.

Why Horizontal Gene Transfer (HGT) Is So Much Rarer in Eukaryotes Compared to Prokaryotes


1. Quantitative Comparison of HGT Frequency


The stark difference in HGT frequency between prokaryotes and eukaryotes is well-documented:


  • Bacteria: On average, 81% of genes in bacterial genomes have been involved in HGT [1]. HGT is so pervasive in prokaryotes that it is considered a primary driver of their genome evolution, with some gene families being shared across distantly related species through cross-phylum transfers [2].
  • Eukaryotes: In nuclear genomes, "at most, only a few percent of genes are detectably derived from HGT, which is several-fold lower than in most bacterial and archaeal genomes" [3]. The average for protists is about 1% of gene inventories [4]. For animals, the record holder is the bdelloid rotifer with ~8% of genes from bacterial origins [5].


This means eukaryotic HGT is roughly 10–100 times rarer per gene than in prokaryotes.


---


2. Biological Barriers to HGT in Eukaryotes


2.1 The Nuclear Envelope: A Physical Barrier


In prokaryotes, there is no physical barrier between the cell's interior and the environment — foreign DNA can enter directly. In eukaryotes, the nuclear envelope creates a critical additional step. After foreign DNA enters the cytoplasm, it must be actively transported through the nuclear pore complex (NPC). DNA molecules larger than ~9 nm cannot passively diffuse through the NPC and require active nuclear import [6]. This double-membrane structure represents a significant physical filter that most foreign DNA cannot pass. A 2026 study by Zachar, Máté, and Oszoli specifically argued that the cell nucleus itself may have evolved as a defense against HGT, particularly in the context of endosymbioses, by limiting genetic exchange with potential endosymbiont partners [7].


2.2 Germline Segregation (Weissman Barrier)


A major barrier in multicellular eukaryotes, especially animals, is the physical separation of germline cells from somatic cells. In animals, only mutations in the germline are heritable. Foreign DNA that integrates into somatic cells is not transmitted to offspring and is thus evolutionary dead weight. As Keeling and Palmer (2008) noted, "eukaryotes with a highly segregated germ line (that is, animals) will tend to be most sheltered from heritably meaningful exposure to foreign DNA" [8]. The weak-link model (Huang, 2013) proposes that HGT in multicellular eukaryotes is more likely to succeed when it occurs in unicellular or early developmental stages (spores, zygotes, embryos) that are not yet committed to the germline-soma distinction [9]. This model has been supported by evidence that HGT events in animals often occur in species with asexual reproduction or in specific tissue types [10][11].


In plants, the situation is somewhat different because somatic cells can sometimes dedifferentiate and regenerate into full organisms, making any cell a potential entry point for heritable HGT [6]. However, even in plants, the nuclear envelope and other barriers still apply.


2.3 RNA Interference (RNAi) and Small RNA-Mediated Silencing


Eukaryotes possess sophisticated RNAi-based defense systems that can target and silence foreign nucleic acids:


  • Plants: RNA-directed DNA methylation (RdDM) uses small interfering RNAs (siRNAs) to direct DNA methylation and histone modifications to homologous sequences, creating heterochromatin that silences transposons and potentially foreign DNA. Dicer-like (DCL) proteins process double-stranded RNA into 20–26 nt siRNAs, which are loaded into Argonaute (AGO) protein complexes to cleave target RNAs [12]. This system has been described as a "natural immune system" in plants [12].


  • Animals (germline-specific): The piRNA pathway operates specifically in animal germlines to protect genome integrity. PIWI-piRNA complexes silence transposable elements and foreign genetic elements both transcriptionally (by recruiting repressive chromatin modifications) and post-transcriptionally (by cleaving target transcripts) [13]. The piRNA pathway has been compared to an "adaptive immune system" because it stores memory of previous transposon invasions in piRNA clusters and can specifically respond to new elements [13]. In C. elegans, the 21U-RNA pathway can recognize and silence "new foreign elements of any sequence" [13], suggesting a broad surveillance function.


  • General defense: In both plants and animals, RNAi serves to control gene expression, maintain genome stability, and defend against viral infections and transposable elements [14]. When exogenous DNA is introduced, the cell's RNAi machinery can generate small RNAs that target and silence this foreign material.


2.4 Chromatin-Based Silencing (DNA Methylation and Histone Modifications)


Eukaryotic genomes are organized into euchromatin (transcriptionally active) and heterochromatin (transcriptionally silent, highly condensed). Foreign DNA that integrates into regions of heterochromatin is effectively silenced. The interplay between:


  • DNA methylation (5-methylcytosine in plants and animals)
  • Histone modifications (H3K9 methylation = silencing; H3K4 methylation = activation)
  • RdDM (which uses siRNAs to direct both DNA methylation and histone modification)


...creates a powerful system for silencing non-native DNA. Studies in mouse models have shown that silencing of transgene expression is "accompanied by an increase in heterochromatin-associated histone modifications" (e.g., H3K9me3) and a decrease in euchromatin marks (e.g., H3K4me3) [15]. This epigenetic silencing is a key mechanism for preventing the expression of foreign DNA, which is a necessary first step for HGT to be functionally significant.


2.5 Cellular Complexity: The "Complexity Hypothesis"


A key concept in understanding the HGT gap is the complexity hypothesis. The idea, first proposed by Koonin, is that the probability of a transferred gene being functionally integrated and fixed in a recipient genome is inversely correlated with the complexity of the gene product's interactions with the cellular network [8]. Bacterial genes, which often function in simple metabolic pathways, can be easily incorporated. Eukaryotic genes, by contrast, typically participate in complex protein-protein interaction networks, regulatory cascades, and multi-protein complexes. A foreign gene that cannot properly interact with the host's proteome is either not expressed, is misfolded, or disrupts normal cellular function, and is therefore selected against. This was confirmed by Burch et al. (2023), who found a "significant interaction between divergence and connectivity" in bacteria, where the ability of a transferred gene to engage in normal protein-protein interactions is a key determinant of HGT success [16]. The same logic applies afortiori in eukaryotes, which have more complex interaction networks.


Keeling (2024) extends this with a "chance and necessity" argument, suggesting that in eukaryotes, the necessity of a foreign gene (i.e., whether it provides a useful function) is a more important factor than the chance of initial transfer, because eukaryotes have phagocytosis and endosymbiosis that provide many genes from other sources, reducing the evolutionary need for HGT [3].


2.6 Multicellularity: The Somatic Cell Shield


In addition to the nuclear envelope, the very fact of being a multicellular organism adds multiple layers of difficulty. For a foreign gene to be fixed in a eukaryotic population, it must: (1) enter a cell, (2) survive as an extrachromosomal element, (3) integrate into the genome, (4) be conserved through cell division, (5) be present in a cell that can give rise to the next generation, and (6) be stably maintained in the population. Each of these steps represents a potential failure point. The biorxiv eukaryotic HGT dataset preprint (2025) notes that "Somatic cells may shield the germline from foreign DNA, decreasing the chance of foreign DNA being transmitted to the progeny" [17]. The only scenario in which a somatic cell can successfully contribute a gene to the next generation is if the organism can reproduce asexually (budding, fission) or if the somatic cell also functions as a gamete (as in some basal eukaryotes) [3][6].


By contrast, in prokaryotes, any cell that acquires a gene can pass it to all its descendants, and the high rate of recombination makes even single successful events significant.


2.7 Absence of Natural Genetic Transformation Machinery


Prokaryotes have evolved three well-characterized, naturally occurring mechanisms for gene transfer: conjugation (direct cell-to-cell contact), transformation (uptake of free DNA from the environment), and transduction (bacteriophage-mediated transfer) [18]. Eukaryotes have not been observed to use prokaryote-type conjugation, transformation, or transduction, and no eukaryotic proteins or protein complexes have been identified that would mediate these processes [18]. The only known natural mechanism for new gene entry into eukaryotic nuclear genomes is endosymbiotic gene transfer (from organelles) and, in the case of some parasites, from endosymbionts [10]. The Agrobacterium T-DNA transfer to plant cells (a type of "naturally occurring genetic engineering") is a notable exception, but this is a pathogen-driven event, not a general eukaryotic mechanism.


2.8 The 70% Rule: A Genomic Standard


Ku and Martin (2016) applied a strict criterion: for a prokaryote-to-eukaryote HGT to be real, a eukaryotic gene would need to show >70% amino acid identity to a prokaryotic homolog. They found that "genes in eukaryotic genomes that share ≥70% amino acid identity to prokaryotic homologs are genome-specific; that is, they are not found outside individual genome assemblies" [18] — i.e., they are likely assembly or annotation artifacts, not true HGT. This "70% rule" supports the conclusion that genuine, recent prokaryote-to-eukaryote HGT is extremely rare in eukaryotic nuclear genomes, in contrast to the continuous, ongoing gene flow seen among prokaryotes.


---


3. How HGT Does Occur in Eukaryotes: Overcoming the Barriers


Despite these barriers, HGT in eukaryotes is not impossible, and several strategies can overcome them:


  • Wolbachia endosymbiont transfers in arthropods: The close physical association of Wolbachia with host gametes in the cytoplasm of oocytes provides a direct route. In Drosophila ananassae, the entire ~1.4 Mbp Wolbachia genome was integrated [10]. The "70% of infected hosts" estimate for nuclear Wolbachia inserts [10] suggests this is a common event in some lineages.


  • Asexually reproducing organisms: Bdelloid rotifers, which have not had a known sexual phase in 40+ million years, show ~8% HGT from various sources, with foreign DNA concentrated in telomeric regions [10]. Asexuality removes the need to pass through a normal meiotic germline.


  • Unicellular eukaryotes and early embryonic stages: The "weak-link model" predicts that HGT is more common in protists, fungi, and in the early developmental stages of multicellular organisms, when the nuclear-cytoplasmic and germline-soma boundaries are not yet fully established [9][11].


  • Type IV secretion systems: In plant parasitic nematodes, a Meloidogyne incognita acquired 60 genes for cell wall-degrading enzymes from bacteria, allowing it to exploit plant hosts. These functional HGTs have been biochemically confirmed [10].


  • Intentional T-DNA transfer by Agrobacterium: In nature, the plant pathogen A. tumefaciens deliberately transfers 10–30 kbp of T-DNA to plant cells, and this has been harnessed for plant genetic engineering [10].

5. Caveats and Uncertainties


  • The 81% HGT figure for prokaryotes and the 1% for eukaryotes are order-of-magnitude estimates that depend on detection methods, taxon sampling, and the time frame of analysis. Newer data for eukaryotes (e.g., the 2022 biorxiv preprint) found that 90% of eukaryotes contain some HGT, but the number of genes involved per eukaryote is very small [1].
  • The "70% rule" of Ku and Martin (2016) is a methodological standard; it is debated whether this stringency is too high, possibly excluding some real HGT events [18].
  • HGT in eukaryotes is an active area of research. The more eukaryotic genomes that are sequenced, the more HGT events are discovered, particularly in unicellular eukaryotes and in organisms with unusual biology (parasites, symbionts, asexual lineages).
  • Some researchers argue that HGT may be more common than detected due to rapid degradation of foreign sequences and limited systematic screening of multicellular eukaryotic genomes [8][11].

Evolutionary Significance and Adaptive Benefits of Horizontal Gene Transfer (HGT) in Eukaryotes


Executive Summary


Horizontal gene transfer (HGT), once considered a mechanism exclusive to prokaryotes, is now recognized as a significant evolutionary force in eukaryotes. While its frequency is lower than in bacteria, HGT events in eukaryotes—when they occur—often confer substantial selective advantages that enable adaptation to new ecological niches, exploitation of novel resources, and survival under environmental stress. The adaptive benefits include metabolic pathway acquisition, detoxification capabilities, defense against pathogens, nutritional optimization, and facilitation of major ecological transitions.


---


1. Conceptual Framework: Barriers and Entry Points for HGT in Eukaryotes


The "weak-link model" proposes that HGT in multicellular eukaryotes occurs at weakly protected stages of the lifecycle, such as spores, zygotes, embryos, or exposed gametes [1]. The primary barrier in animals is the physical isolation of germ cells from somatic cells, but this is not insurmountable [1]. In plants, early developmental stages (archegonia, exposed male gametes) and parasitic plant connections provide entry points [1][2]. Asexual reproduction also facilitates HGT, as any cell carrying foreign DNA can propagate it into offspring [1].


Proposed facilitators of interkingdom HGT include viruses, transposable elements, extracellular membrane vesicles, and tunneling nanotubes, though only Agrobacterium-mediated transfer has been directly observed as a natural mechanism [3].


---


2. Specific Adaptive Benefits of HGT in Plants


2.1 Land Colonization (Phenylpropanoid Pathway)


The most iconic example of adaptive HGT in plants involves the phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL) gene. Emiliani et al. (2009) demonstrated through robust phylogenetic analysis that the ancestor of land plants acquired PAL via HGT from soil bacteria during early symbioses with fungi and bacteria [4]. This enzyme catalyzes the first step of the phenylpropanoid pathway, producing flavonoids and lignin precursors essential for UV protection, desiccation resistance, and structural support on land [4]. Without this HGT event, the phenylpropanoid pathway could not have emerged in land plants [4].


2.2 Comprehensive Gene Acquisition for Terrestrial Adaptation


Cheng et al. (2012) identified 57 families of nuclear genes acquired by the moss Physcomitrella patens from prokaryotes, fungi, or viruses [5]. These genes are involved in:


  • Xylem formation (vascular development)
  • Plant defense (acid phosphatase genes against herbivorous insects)
  • Stress tolerance (cadmium stress via argC; cold stress via HAD-superfamily hydrolase)
  • Biosynthesis of glutathione (γ-glutamylcysteine ligase from α-proteobacteria), auxin (YUC3), starch, polyamines, hormones
  • Nitrogen recycling (purine degradation pathways)
  • Isoprenoid biosynthesis (methionine gamma-lyase for isoleucine under osmotic/drought stress) [5]


These findings demonstrate that HGT had a critical role in the transition of plants from aquatic to terrestrial environments [5].


2.3 Defense Against Plant Pathogens


The wheat resistance gene Fhb7 was acquired from the endophytic fungus Epichloe via HGT. This glutathione-S-transferase gene enables wheat to detoxify trichothecene toxins produced by Fusarium species, providing resistance against Fusarium head blight [6].


2.4 Detoxification of Plant Chemical Defenses (Plant-to-Insect HGT)


An extraordinary case involves the plant-derived BtPMaT1 gene (phenolic glucoside malonyltransferase) acquired by whiteflies (Bemisia tabaci) [7][8]. This gene enables whiteflies to neutralize phenolic glycoside defense compounds produced by host plants, explaining their remarkable polyphagous nature across diverse plant species [7][8]. The transfer likely occurred between 35–80 million years ago [9].


2.5 Cell Wall Biosynthesis


A viral cellulose synthase gene was acquired by streptophyte ancestors (precursors to all land plants), correlated with the evolution of cellulose-based cell walls [6].


2.6 Metabolic Pathway Replacement


In marine pennate diatoms (Pseudo-nitzschia spp.), ferritin genes used for iron storage are more closely related to archaeal homologs than to other plant genes, suggesting replacement through HGT [10].


---


3. Specific Adaptive Benefits of HGT in Animals


3.1 Arthropod Herbivory Adaptation


HGT has been extensively documented in phytophagous arthropods, enabling them to overcome nutritional and defensive barriers imposed by plants [11]:


  • Plant cell wall-degrading enzymes (PCWDEs): Beetles (Phytophaga clade) acquired multiple genes for cellulases, hemicellulases, and pectate lyases from microbes, enabling specialization on wood and seeds [11].
  • Carotenoid biosynthesis: Aphids and spider mites acquired carotenoid biosynthesis genes from fungi, enabling synthesis of pigments with roles in photoprotection and potentially solar-powered energy metabolism [12][13].
  • Cyanide detoxification: Found in phytophagous mites and lepidopterans [14].
  • β-fructofuranosidases: Acquired by Lepidoptera for carbohydrate assimilation [11].
  • Mannanase (coffee borer beetle Hypothenemus hampei): Enables exclusive feeding on coffee beans [14].


These transfers often undergo subsequent duplication and functional diversification, enabling colonization of additional host plant species [11].


3.2 Innate Immunity and Pathogen Defense


  • Bacterial amidase genes (tae): These peptidoglycan-cleaving genes were domesticated in at least three eukaryotic supergroups over 800 million years ago. Experimentally verified, they limit Borrelia burgdorferi proliferation in deer ticks (Ixodes scapularis) [14].
  • Aerolysin pore-forming toxins: Transferred independently to numerous eukaryotes; hypothesized to be involved in prey disintegration in Hydra, blood cell lysis by ticks, and water stress regulation in plants [14].
  • Nonribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPS) and polyketide synthetases (PKS): Acquired by bdelloid rotifers for antimicrobial production [15].


3.3 Bdelloid Rotifer Defense Mechanism


Bdelloid rotifers deploy horizontally acquired biosynthetic genes as a unique defense strategy. When challenged with fungal pathogens, HGT-derived genes are over twice as likely to be upregulated compared to other genes. The resistant species Adineta ricciae upregulates NRPS/PKS clusters (putative antibiotic producers) nearly ten times more strongly than susceptible species [15]. These bacteria-derived biosynthetic pathways represent a novel antimicrobial defense mechanism absent from other animals [15].


3.4 Nematode Plant Parasitism


Plant-parasitic nematodes have acquired multiple gene families via HGT from bacteria and fungi, including cellulases, pectate lyases, xylanases, expansin-like proteins, chorismate mutase, cyanate lyase, NodL-like genes, vitamin biosynthesis genes, and invertase [16]. These genes serve four functional roles: (i) modification/degradation of plant cell walls, (ii) suppression of host defenses, (iii) establishment of feeding sites, and (iv) nutrient biosynthesis [16]. The widespread occurrence across independently evolved plant-parasitic lineages suggests HGT may be a prerequisite for successful plant parasitism in nematodes [16].


---


4. Specific Adaptive Benefits of HGT in Fungi


Fungi exhibit particularly high rates of HGT due to their extracellular heterotrophic lifestyle, which makes them metabolically "bacterial-like" [17]. Examples include:


  • Toxin gene transfer: An 11 kb region containing a toxin gene transferred to a previously avirulent fungal species approximately 70 years ago, causing emergence of a new disease [18].
  • Convergent plant pathogenesis: 11 genes from filamentous fungi transferred to oomycetes, contributing to convergent adaptation to plant pathogenesis via osmotrophy [18].
  • Alkaloid biosynthesis: The mcf gene (makes caterpillars floppy), acquired from bacterial HGT, enables Epichloe fungi to produce insect-killing alkaloids [14].
  • Endosymbiont compensation: In sap-feeding insects (aphids, psyllids, whiteflies, mealybugs), HGT from Wolbachia and Cardinium compensates for genome reduction in their nutritional endosymbionts [14].


---


5. Specific Adaptive Benefits of HGT in Protists


  • Anaerobic metabolism: Anaerobic parasites (Giardia, Trichomonas, Entamoeba) acquired numerous bacterial genes related to fermentation and anaerobic metabolism [18].
  • Thermoacidophilic adaptation: The red alga Galdieria sulphuraria accumulated ~5% of its genome through HGT from extremophile bacteria and archaea, enabling survival in hot, acidic, metal-rich volcanic environments [19][20].
  • Ice-binding proteins: Bacterial genes encoding ice-binding proteins were transferred to polar algae (Fragilariopsis cylindrus, Phaeocystis antarctica), enabling survival in extremely cold environments [14].
  • Soil adaptation: Dictyostelium discoideum acquired bacterial genes for dipeptidases, siderophores, and soil toxin resistance [18].


---


6. The Debate: How Common Is Adaptive HGT in Multicellular Eukaryotes?


There is ongoing debate about the frequency and significance of HGT in multicellular eukaryotes:


Arguments for significance:


  • Specific well-documented cases with experimental validation (tae genes in ticks, PAL in plants, Fhb7 in wheat, BtPMaT1 in whiteflies) [4][6][7][14][15]
  • Recent reviews argue HGT has been critically important for plant terrestrialization [5]
  • Models predict HGT should be higher than currently detected [1]


Arguments for caution:


  • Many reported HGT cases cannot be distinguished from ancestral gene loss, incomplete lineage sorting, or contamination [3]
  • False positives are common in phylogenetic analyses
  • Some researchers propose the "70% rule": coding sequences below 70% identity are likely misattributed HGT [21]
  • The majority of eukaryotic genome evolution still relies on vertical inheritance
  • Most acquired genes appear non-functional or transient [17]

8. Conclusions


HGT provides significant evolutionary advantages to eukaryotes when it occurs, primarily by enabling rapid acquisition of complex traits that would otherwise require lengthy incremental mutation. The most dramatic examples involve:


  1. Facilitating major ecological transitions (land colonization by plants via PAL; adaptation to anaerobic environments)
  2. Enabling exploitation of new food sources (cellulases in beetles; carotenoid synthesis in aphids)
  3. Detoxification and defense (cyanide detoxification; aerolysins; NRPS/PKS antibiotics in rotifers)
  4. Overcoming biotic barriers (plant defense neutralization in whiteflies; nematode cell wall-degrading enzymes)


While HGT is less frequent in multicellular eukaryotes than in prokaryotes, the evidence increasingly supports its role as a genuine alternative pathway for adaptive innovation—particularly in organisms with flexible developmental biology, close microbial associations, or asexual reproduction. The exact contribution remains debated, with many cases difficult to verify definitively.

Key Findings


1. The HGT Event


The study provides robust phylogenetic evidence that the ancestor of land plants acquired a phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL) gene via horizontal gene transfer (HGT) from soil bacteria, likely through ancient symbiotic associations with fungi and soil bacteria that were present during early land colonization [1]. The authors identify a clade of bacterial homologues (including cyanobacteria from the order Nostocales, Streptomyces maritimus, Photorhabdus luminescens, Herpetosiphon aurantiacus, and Methylobacterium sp.) as the sister group to the combined land plant/fungi PAL cluster, supporting a bacterial origin for the gene [1].


2. Direction of Transfer


The proposed scenario involves a stepwise transfer: PAL emerged in bacteria → a member of an early fungal lineage (likely Dikarya) obtained PAL via HGT from a bacterium → this fungal PAL was transferred to an ancestor of land plants via an ancient arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) symbiosis [1]. This route through fungi is favored because AM symbioses are known to have established very early during the first steps of land colonization.


3. Why PAL Was Essential


The authors demonstrate that land plants lack histidine ammonia lyase (HAL), which is present in most other eukaryotes. No HAL orthologues were found in any sequenced member of the phylum Plantae (red algae, green algae, or angiosperms), indicating that the HAL gene was lost early in the plant lineage [1]. Since HAL could not perform the same function as PAL, the acquisition of a functional PAL via HGT was the only way for the plant ancestor to access the phenylpropanoid pathway.

PAL catalyzes the deamination of L-phenylalanine to trans-cinnamic acid, which is the first committed step of the phenylpropanoid pathway. This pathway produces critical compounds necessary for terrestrial life:


  • Flavonoids (from p-coumaroyl-CoA): essential for UV radiation protection (as pigments) and antimicrobial defense [1]
  • Lignin monomers (from p-coumaric acid/p-coumaroyl-CoA): essential for vascularization (xylem development) and stem rigidity out of water [1]
  • Antimicrobial metabolites: cinnamate and p-coumarate may have served as direct antimicrobial agents in the soil environment [1]

Citation: Aubin, E., El Baidouri, M., & Panaud, O. (2021). "Horizontal Gene Transfers in Plants." Life 11(8): 857. DOI: 10.3390/life11080857.


This review article published in the journal Life (MDPI) provides a comprehensive overview of the current state of knowledge on horizontal gene transfer (HGT) in plants, organized into sections covering parasitism, grafting, food chain interactions, other routes, and transposable element transfers.


---


1. How Common is HGT in Plants?


The review concludes that HGT is now "widely admitted" as being widespread in eukaryotes, including plants. The advent of next-generation sequencing (NGS) technologies has dramatically accelerated discoveries through comparative genomic approaches [1]. Hundreds of reports have evidenced HGTs involving protists and multicellular organisms including plants, animals, and fungi, demonstrating that "gene flows among distinct taxa occur frequently within ecosystems" — contradicting earlier views that HGT was rare or anecdotal in multicellular organisms [1].


Regarding transposable elements specifically, a genome-wide study by El Baidouri et al. found 32 horizontal transfers of transposable elements (HTTs) among a sample of 40 angiosperm species, from which they extrapolated that "hundreds of thousands of HTTs had occurred among flowering plants within the last two million years" [1].


Regarding parasites specifically, Kado and Innan estimated that 0.1–0.2% of genes in obligate parasitic plants originated from HGTs from their hosts, and that large genomic regions (more than 100 kbp) were transferred at once [1].


---


2. Mechanisms That Facilitate HGT in Plants


The review identifies several key mechanisms:


a) Parasitism (haustoria-mediated)


The intimate association between parasitic plants and their hosts provides a direct route for HGT. Parasitic plants form vascular connections with host plants through haustoria, which enable transfer of water, nutrients, proteins, mRNAs, and even genomic DNA [1]. Mower et al. were the first to report an HGT (mitochondrial atp1 gene) between the parasitic genera Cuscuta and Bartsia and several Plantago species. Since then, multiple studies have confirmed that HGTs of mitochondrial genes between parasitic plants and their hosts are frequent [1]. Yang et al. showed that HGTs in Cuscuta originated from movement of genomic DNA [1].


b) Grafting


Grafting, the joining of vascular tissues of two different plants, has been known as a horticultural practice for centuries. It has proven to be a powerful mechanism for inter-species genetic exchange. Stegemann and Bock demonstrated that complete genetic material could fuse between grafted mutants. Stegemann et al. further showed that a complete chloroplast could be transferred through grafting between two Nicotiana species. Fuentes et al. showed that allopolyploid plants could be regenerated from calli at the graft site, suggesting that grafting can be the origin of new polyploid species [1].


c) Other Routes


  • Food chain / "You are what you eat" hypothesis: The most famous example is the sea slug Elysia chlorotica becoming photosynthetic by feeding on algae, though this is not transgenerational and thus not a true HGT in the strictest sense [1].
  • Unexplained cases: Many HGTs, especially of organellar (mitochondrial) and some nuclear DNA, are observed without any clear mechanistic explanation. A particularly striking case is the entire genome of several algae and a moss being transferred to the Amborella angiosperm mitochondria, creating a 3.9 Mbp organelle (six times the average size in flowering plants) [1]. The mechanism of such large transfers may involve whole-mitochondria fusion [1].
  • The review notes that the most important open question is how HGTs can occur "between species with no obvious biological relationships" and whether parasites may act as "genetic bridges" to spread adaptive genes among sympatric species [1].


d) Transposable Element Horizontal Transfers


TEs are particularly prone to HGT because they can exist as extrachromosomal forms during their transposition cycle, making them more likely to be transferred than chromosomal genes. TEs are also often triggered by biotic stress, and the review speculates that pathogen attacks may favor HTT, though this remains to be tested [1].


---


3. Evolutionary Significance of HGT in Plants


The review documents multiple examples of HGT conferring new adaptive functions:


a) Metabolic and Physiological Adaptations


  • C4 photosynthesis: The PPC gene (phosphoenolpyruvate carboxylase, a C4 carbon-fixing enzyme) has been transferred multiple times in plants, giving rise to independent acquisitions of C4 photosynthetic metabolism. A survey of the grass Alloteropsis semialata and 146 related species found 59 HGT events involving at least nine donor species [1].
  • Low-light adaptation: Ferns adapted to low-light conditions under angiosperm canopies acquired a neochrome gene via HGT from bryophytes (~179 Mya) [1].
  • Land colonization: The moss Physcomitrella patens shows evidence of multiple HGTs associated with land colonization, including genes for xylem formation, plant defense, nitrogen recycling, and biosynthesis of starch, polyamines, hormones, and glutathione [1].
  • Disease resistance: The Fhb7 gene in wheat, which confers resistance to Fusarium head blight, originated from an HGT between the Epichloë fungus and Thinopyrum elongatum [1].


b) Intergenomic/Intergeneric Transfers


  • Insect-plant transfer (whitefly): Bemisia tabaci (the whitefly) acquired a plant-derived phenolic glucoside malonyltransferase gene (BtPMaT1), which allows it to neutralize plant toxins [1].
  • Insect-plant transfer (cacao): Two Transferrin genes were transferred from insects to Theobroma cacao, potentially contributing to functions related to iron homeostasis, immunity, cell growth, and differentiation [1].


c) Intergenic/Kingdom Transfers


  • Antifungal peptides: Drosomycin-type antifungal peptides (DTAFPs), widespread in plants, have been transferred to animals via HGT [1].
  • Desiccation resistance: Four trehalose-6-phosphate synthase (TPS) genes in rotifers involved in desiccation resistance are of plant origin [1].


---


4. Remaining Challenges and Future Directions


The review emphasizes several unresolved questions:


  1. Mechanism of non-obvious transfers: Understanding how HGT occurs between species with no biological relationships remains the "next challenge."
  2. Parasites as vectors: Whether parasites serve as "genetic bridges" to spread adaptive genes among sympatric species within or across kingdoms.
  3. Detection methods: Developing improved methods combining genome-wide similarity searches with transcriptomic data to detect recent biologically relevant HGTs.
  4. In situ ecology: New sequencing technologies with higher throughput and lower costs should enable population genomics approaches to quantify the extent and ecological role of interspecific gene flow in natural ecosystems [1].


---


Key Takeaway


HGT in plants is now recognized as a common and ecologically significant phenomenon, not a rare exception. It is primarily facilitated by direct physical contact (parasitism, grafting) and the movement of transposable elements, and it has demonstrable evolutionary consequences — from enabling major metabolic innovations (C4 photosynthesis) to conferring specific adaptive traits (disease resistance, detoxification, desiccation tolerance). The field is still in an early stage of discovery, with NGS-based approaches expected to reveal even more HGT events in the future.

Horizontal Gene Transfer (HGT) in Plants and Animals: A Comprehensive Analysis


Executive Summary


Horizontal gene transfer—the movement of genetic material between organisms that are not in a parent-offspring relationship—was once considered a phenomenon exclusive to prokaryotes. However, genomic advances over the past two decades have revealed that HGT occurs across all domains of life, including multicellular plants and animals. While far less frequent than in bacteria (~81% of bacterial genes show evidence of past HGT [1]), HGT in eukaryotes remains a genuine evolutionary force that has shaped major biological transitions and conferred adaptive advantages.


---


1. Prevalence and Mechanisms of HGT in Plants


Key Documented Cases


Parasitic plant-to-host transfers: The first documented plant HGT involved Cuscuta (dodder) transferring mitochondrial genes (atp1, atp6, matR) to host Plantago species [2]. Dodder subsequently acquired 108 functional nuclear genes from host plants, contributing to haustoria development, defense responses, and amino acid metabolism [3].


Host-to-parasite nuclear gene transfer: Yoshida et al. (2010) demonstrated that the parasitic weed Striga hermonthica captured nuclear genes from its grass hosts (e.g., sorghum), representing the first evidence of nuclear gene capture by parasitic weeds from hosts in nature [4].


Agrobacterium T-DNA transfer: At least 93 naturally transgenic plant species carry integrated Agrobacterium-derived T-DNA sequences, with integration events spanning 0.62–24 million years ago [5][6].


Gymnosperm-to-angiosperm transfer: Won & Renner (2003) discovered mitochondrial nad1 intron/exon transfer from an asterid to Gnetum (gymnosperms). A 2024 study found 36,635–194,613 bp of foreign angiosperm DNA in Asian Gnetum mitogenomes from multiple independent HGT events >10 million years ago [7][8].


Land colonization PAL gene: Emiliani et al. (2009) demonstrated through phylogenetic analysis that the ancestor of land plants acquired phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL) via HGT from soil bacteria, likely mediated through arbuscular mycorrhizal symbioses. This enzyme catalyzes the first step of the phenylpropanoid pathway, which produces flavonoids (for UV protection) and lignin (for structural support)—both essential for terrestrial life. The paper states: "The horizontally acquired PAL represented then the basis for further development of the phenylpropanoid pathway and plant radiation on terrestrial environments" [9].


C4 photosynthesis optimization: In the grass Alloteropsis semialata, 59 functional genes from at least nine donor species were transferred in 23 large DNA fragments (up to 170 kb), including photosynthesis-related genes. These transfers contributed to optimization of C4 photosynthesis over the last ~10 million years [10][11].


Fhb7 disease resistance gene: Wang et al. (2020) demonstrated that the Fhb7 glutathione S-transferase gene in wheat relative Thinopyrum elongatum originated from the endophytic fungus Epichloë. This gene enables enzymatic detoxification of trichothecene mycotoxins produced by Fusarium pathogens, conferring resistance to Fusarium head blight [12].


Neochrome gene (hornworts → ferns): Li et al. (2014) showed that the chimeric photoreceptor gene neochrome was transferred from hornworts to ferns via HGT ~179 million years ago. This adaptation enables ferns to sense both red and blue light under low-light forest understory conditions, contributing to fern diversification [13].


Transposable element transfers: El Baidouri et al. found 32 horizontal transfers of transposable elements among 40 angiosperm species, extrapolating that "hundreds of thousands of HTTs had occurred among flowering plants within the last two million years" [3].

  • Parasitism/haustoria: Direct vascular connections through haustoria enable physical transfer of DNA, proteins, mRNAs, and organelles [3]
  • Grafting: Vascular tissue fusion between grafted species can result in complete organelle (chloroplast) and nuclear genome transfer
  • Endosymbiont relationships (Wolbachia, Agrobacterium)
  • Intergenomic gene flow through food chains

4. Why Is HGT So Rare in Eukaryotes?


Multiple stacked barriers prevent HGT in eukaryotes:


Nuclear envelope: Foreign DNA must cross the double-membrane nuclear envelope via the nuclear pore complex. DNA >9 nm cannot passively diffuse through NPC [25].


Germline segregation: In animals, somatic cells are physically separated from germ cells. Only germline mutations are heritable. Foreign DNA in somatic cells is evolutionary dead weight [26].


RNA interference/silencing: Plants use RNA-directed DNA methylation (RdDM); animals use piRNA pathways in the germline. Both systems target and silence foreign nucleic acids as a natural immune defense [27][28].


Complexity hypothesis: Eukaryotic genes participate in more complex protein-protein interaction networks. A foreign gene that cannot properly integrate into these networks is selected against. Burch et al. (2023) confirmed that the ability of a transferred gene to engage in normal protein-protein interactions is a key determinant of HGT success [29].


Multicellularity: Each additional step—entry, nuclear import, integration, maintenance through division, transmission to offspring—represents a potential failure point [30].


No natural transformation machinery: Unlike bacteria, eukaryotes lack constitutive mechanisms for DNA uptake and recombination [31].


---


5. Evolutionary Significance of Adaptive HGT


Despite its rarity, when HGT occurs in eukaryotes, it often delivers substantial adaptive benefits:


  1. Major ecological transitions: PAL acquisition enabled land colonization by plants [9]; neochrome acquisition enabled ferns to thrive in low-light environments [13].
  2. Metabolic innovation: C4 photosynthesis optimization through repeated gene transfers [10]; novel detoxification pathways (trichothecene GST in wheat; cyanide detoxification in mites; mannanase in coffee borers) [12][20][21].
  3. Herbivory adaptation: Cell wall-degrading enzymes in nematodes; carotenoid biosynthesis in aphids; phenolic glycoside detoxification in whiteflies [18][19][32].
  4. Defense innovation: Aerolysin pore-forming toxins in ticks; antibiotic biosynthetic clusters in bdelloid rotifers [24][33].
  5. Symbiosis establishment: Wolbachia-derived metabolic genes provisioning insect hosts [17].


---


6. Remaining Uncertainties


  • The precise mechanisms facilitating many non-obvious plant-to-plant HGT events remain unclear [3].
  • Whether many transferred genes are truly functional versus selectively neutral is difficult to assess [3].
  • The debate over HGT in the human genome continues, with methodological challenges in distinguishing true HGT from differential gene loss [34].
  • In bdelloid rotifers, the mechanism by which foreign DNA enters the genome (given their obligate asexuality) remains poorly understood [22].
  • Some researchers question whether some candidate HGT regions represent genuine transfers versus incomplete lineage sorting [34].


---


Conclusion


HGT in plants and animals is rare but consequential. It operates through specific biological interfaces (haustoria, endosymbioses, asexual reproduction) that bypass eukaryotic barriers. When successful, HGT delivers genes that confer immediate selective advantages—detoxification capabilities, metabolic innovations, defense mechanisms, and ecological niche expansion. The most dramatic examples include the acquisition of PAL from bacteria enabling plant colonization of land, and the acquisition of carotenoid biosynthesis genes from fungi enabling aphids to synthesize their own pigments. Far from being evolutionarily insignificant, HGT in eukaryotes represents a genuine alternative pathway for adaptive innovation that has shaped the history of life on Earth.
